1. Nazi Regime's Initial Years and Army's Role

During the initial years of Nazi rule from 1933 to 1936, the Nazis dismantled the existing German constitution, eliminated opposition, and started incarcerating 'undesirables' in concentration camps. The most notable event was in June 1934 when Hitler executed numerous political opponents and later became the F?hrer following President Hindenburg's death. The German army, under its leadership, played a passive and sometimes active role in supporting these actions and increasingly subordinated itself to the Nazi regime. This was not only due to the political and ideological reasons that initially led the army to support the Nazis but also because of the significant expansion of the army initiated by the regime. The German army grew from a 100,000-man Reichswehr in 1933 to a potential wartime force of 2.6 million men by 1936, transforming it into a 'people's army' and diluting the conservative nature of the officer corps. This expansion made the army more susceptible to National Socialist influence.

2. Hitler's Ambitious Foreign and Military Policy

Hitler's foreign and military policy was marked by ambitious plans for German expansion and military empowerment, aimed at overturning the Treaty of Versailles and restoring Germany's position among the great nations. His vision extended to unifying German-speaking peoples and achieving Lebensraum (living space) in Eastern Europe for the German people's survival and supremacy. An essential aspect of this strategy was indoctrinating the German populace with National Socialist ideology and militaristic values. This was to be achieved through propaganda, youth organizations, the education system, and the Reich Labour Service (Reichsarbeitsdienst, RAD), with the armed forces finalizing the indoctrination process. Hitler envisioned central Europe as a domain for German domination and economic exploitation, requiring a confrontation with France to dismantle its alliance system in the region. This would pave the way for the most critical phase of his foreign policy: capturing vast territories and resources in Eastern Europe and the Soviet Union, potentially leading to global conflict with powers like the United States and Britain.

3. Key Army Figures: Fritsch, Beck, and Blomberg

The period saw significant contributions from key army figures such as Lieutenant General Werner Freiherr von Fritsch, Major General Ludwig Beck, and General von Blomberg. Fritsch, known for his diligence and controversial views, aligned closely with Nazi ideologies, making collaboration with the regime easier for him. Beck, a proponent of rearmament and national expansion, played a crucial role in developing defensive strategies and advocating for Germany's long-term security and hegemony in central Europe. Blomberg, appointed minister for war and later commander-in-chief of the armed forces, was instrumental in integrating the army with the Nazi regime, driven by his romanticized political beliefs and admiration for totalitarian systems. These figures, among others, facilitated the army's alignment with the Nazi agenda, despite some reservations within the officer corps about the regime's radical elements and the long-term implications of Hitler's expansive foreign policy ambitions.

4. Nazi Economic Program and Rearmament

The Nazi economic program heavily emphasized rearmament, significantly increasing government spending in this sector. This focus not only promised substantial profits for businesses involved but also entailed the destruction of trade unions, aligning with Nazi objectives. Rearmament was initially funded through covert loans from businesses set to benefit, circumventing the Finance Ministry. Projects like the construction of Autobahnen were dual-purpose, serving civilian needs while also catering to military requirements, like rapid troop movement. By March 1935, Hitler openly declared his rearmament intentions, signaling a shift towards more transparent military expansion.

5. Internal Power Struggle and the SA

An internal power struggle within the Nazi movement, particularly between the SA's leadership under Ernst R?hm and the army, highlighted the SA's increasing liability. While the SA's aggression had been instrumental in the Nazis' rise to power, their ongoing lawlessness posed a threat to public order and the economy. R?hm's aspirations to merge the SA with the Reichswehr alarmed the military leadership, given the SA's larger size and radical elements. This conflict culminated in the Night of the Long Knives, where R?hm and other perceived threats were eliminated, realigning the power dynamics within the Nazi regime and reinforcing the army's position.

6. Army's Loyalty Oath to Hitler

Following President Hindenburg's death and Hitler's consolidation of power as the F?hrer, the army leadership crafted an oath of loyalty to Hitler, binding the military to him personally. Despite some reservations from figures like Fritsch and Beck, this oath was widely accepted within the military, symbolizing the deepening alliance between the army and the Nazi regime. This act further blurred the lines between state and military, entwining the army's identity with Hitler's leadership and National Socialist ideals.

7. Expansion and Transformation of the Army

The army underwent significant expansion and transformation, with Hitler's announcement of the end of Versailles Treaty restrictions and the introduction of conscription. The envisioned peacetime force of thirty-six divisions marked a significant increase in the army's size and capability. This expansion not only altered the army's composition, with a focus on infantry and armored forces but also its character, transitioning towards a "people's army" reflective of broader German society. This shift posed challenges in officer recruitment and training, leading to efforts to attract young recruits and streamline the officer corps with Nazi ideology. The integration of police forces with the military, particularly the SS, further exemplified the merging of state and military apparatus under Nazi control.

8. Diversification of the Officer Corps

The German officer corps saw a significant transformation in its composition during the 1930s, reflecting a broader cross-section of German society than ever before. Although aristocratic and upper-middle-class backgrounds remained overrepresented, there was a notable increase in officers from pro-Nazi white-collar families, reflecting the regime's pervasive influence. The rapid expansion of the army and the resulting casualties of war eventually eroded the distinct, long-established officer ethos, making way for a new mentality that aligned with the National Socialist worldview. This shift was emblematic of the broader changes within the German military and society under Nazi rule.

9. Nazi Indoctrination of the Military

The Nazi regime, with Field Marshal Werner von Blomberg playing a significant role, undertook extensive efforts to align the military closely with National Socialist ideology. Measures included the exclusion of Jews and the requirement for officers to engage in "national political teaching," aimed at fostering a unity between military service and Nazi beliefs. This indoctrination extended to making religious services optional and banning soldiers from engaging with Jewish businesses, further entrenching antisemitism within the ranks. Despite these efforts, the full-scale inculcation of ideological fanaticism into the troops would only be aggressively pursued in the war's final years.

10. Public Sentiment Towards Nazism

While Nazi propaganda boasted of a unified and ideologically homogenous Volksgemeinschaft, the reality was more nuanced. Economic focus on rearmament did not translate to widespread personal prosperity, leading to various degrees of public discontent. Despite this, Nazism succeeded in integrating a significant portion of the German populace, offering a sense of order, employment, and national pride that was absent during the Weimar years. This integration fostered a degree of acquiescence, if not outright support, for the regime's discriminatory policies and ambitions.

11. Youth Organizations and Militarization

The Nazi regime heavily invested in youth organizations like the Hitler Youth and the Reich Labour Service (RAD) to instill militaristic and ideological values from a young age. These organizations, along with specialized groups like the National Socialist Motor Vehicle Corps (NSKK), prepared German youth for future military service, promoting physical fitness, practical skills, and a strong sense of comradeship. The widespread participation in these groups ensured that the army's influx of young recruits was already conditioned to the regime's militaristic and ideological demands, further blurring the lines between civilian life and military service.

12. Training and Organization of the New Army

The German army maintained a highly structured training regimen, emphasizing physical rigor and discipline. Recruits underwent 16 weeks of basic training with a focus on endurance and combat readiness. The training process was designed to foster cohesion and instill a sense of duty, albeit sometimes through harsh and dehumanizing methods. This system ensured that soldiers, once assigned to their parent divisions, were well-prepared for the demands of military life. The regional organization of military districts contributed to the cohesiveness of units, as soldiers often served alongside others from their own areas.

13. Emphasis on Auftragstaktik and Independent Thinking

The German military doctrine of Auftragstaktik encouraged flexibility, initiative, and independent decision-making among both officers and NCOs. Training aimed to cultivate these qualities, preparing German soldiers for the unpredictable nature of combat. This approach was reflected in the comprehensive training manuals and directives that emphasized the importance of leadership, education, and tactical adaptability. The rigorous training ensured that German infantry squads were proficient in utilizing the terrain, selecting targets, and executing coordinated maneuvers, making them formidable opponents.

14. Development of German Armoured Warfare

The formation of the first Panzer divisions marked a significant evolution in German military strategy, drawing on experiences from the First World War and early mechanized experiments. These divisions were self-contained units capable of independent operation, embodying the principles of combined-arms warfare. Training for Panzer crews emphasized teamwork and proficiency in both infantry tactics and specialized armored operations. The structure and doctrine of these divisions laid the foundation for the highly effective use of armor in blitzkrieg tactics during the early years of World War II.

15. Contributions to Panzer Tactics and Misconceptions

While figures like Heinz Guderian are often credited with the development of German armored tactics, the reality is that many individuals contributed to these innovations. Oswald Lutz, for instance, played a pivotal role in shaping the early Panzer force and advocating for an independent, flexible strike arm. Contrary to Guderian's assertions, there was a broader acceptance of and enthusiasm for armored warfare within the German military hierarchy. The development of Panzer tactics was a collective effort, influenced by a range of experiences and ideas rather than the vision of a single individual.

16. Panzer Division Development and Armored Warfare Strategy

The early Panzer divisions, primarily comprising light Mark I and II tanks, laid the groundwork for Germany's armored warfare capabilities. Despite their limitations compared to later models, these tanks were integral to the development of combined-arms tactics. The emphasis was on a balanced force capable of independent operations, which would become a hallmark of German military strategy. This approach evolved over the 1930s, leading to the production of more advanced medium tanks like the Mark III and IV, which offered upgradeable armor and armament, crucial for the war's demands.

17. General Staff Training and Organizational Challenges

The German army's General Staff underwent rigorous training, focusing on operational planning. However, there were notable gaps in broader strategic education, including diplomacy and logistics, as well as a lack of detailed training in military intelligence. Additionally, the army faced internal disputes with the Wehrmachtsamt, led by General von Blomberg, over the control and direction of military policy. These turf wars, particularly between the army and central Wehrmacht offices, threatened the unity and effectiveness of the high command, complicating the army's ability to present a unified front to external adversaries.

18. Army's Integration with the Nazi Regime

Between 1933 and 1936, the German army leadership increasingly aligned with the Nazi regime, motivated by the prospects of conscription, rearmament, and the mobilization of society towards militaristic goals. This partnership was driven by the desire to restore Germany's status as a great power, leveraging the state's full support. Despite some generals' reservations about the more radical aspects of Nazism or the socio-economic background of its leaders, the broader vision of a powerful, technologically advanced military overshadowed these concerns. This period marked the beginning of the army's deeper complicity in the regime's broader objectives and eventual wartime atrocities.
