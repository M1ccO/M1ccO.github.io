1. The German Occupation's Dual Approach

During 1942 and the early months of 1943, the German military's occupation strategy in Western and Southeast Europe was marked by a stark contradiction. On one hand, there was a concerted effort to maintain amicable relations with collaborationist governments in regions like France and Belgium, aiming to keep the local populace pacified and cooperative. This strategy was partly driven by a recognition within some sectors of the Nazi leadership that the support of occupied European populations was crucial, especially after the devastating defeat at Stalingrad. The propaganda machinery, led by figures such as Goebbels, was mobilized to portray the conflict as an "anti-Bolshevik crusade," rallying European nations against the perceived threat of Soviet communism.

2. Intensified Economic Exploitation

The German occupation's shift towards economic exploitation had dire consequences for the local economies and populations. In France, for example, the imposition of compulsory labor measures was a significant factor contributing to public unrest. High-ranking officials, notably G?ring, placed paramount importance on extracting resources and labor from these regions to fuel the German war machine, often at the expense of the local populations' wellbeing, by offering high wages and benefits to attract voluntary labor. Despite initial success, the voluntary approach soon proved insufficient, leading to the imposition of compulsory labor measures by 1942. Men and unmarried women were required to work a certain number of hours per week, with severe restrictions on job mobility. This coercive mobilization, coupled with the systematic pillaging of resources, severely strained the economies and daily lives of the occupied territories, exacerbating hardships and fueling resistance movements.

3. Emergence and Escalation of Resistance

The economic hardships and oppressive occupation policies inadvertently fueled the flames of resistance across the occupied territories. In France, for instance, the imposition of compulsory labor measures and the harsh exploitation of the economy led to significant public unrest and the strengthening of resistance movements. The situation was further compounded in the Balkans, where brutal counterinsurgency campaigns by the German military failed to quell the rising tide of guerrilla warfare, particularly in Yugoslavia. These resistance efforts were characterized by fierce determination and were bolstered by the occupiers' increasingly harsh and desperate tactics.

4. The Final Solution's Terrifying Progress

Amidst the backdrop of economic exploitation and growing resistance, the period also witnessed the horrifying escalation of the Final Solution?the Nazi regime's systematic attempt to exterminate the Jewish population of Europe. This genocidal campaign reached a chilling efficiency during these years, with the deportation and murder of countless Jews from various occupied countries to extermination camps. The involvement of military and police forces in these atrocities, alongside the complicity of collaborationist governments, underscored the dark and inhumane dimensions of the German occupation.

5. Worsening Economic Conditions in France

By 1943, France faced severe economic burdens due to German exploitation and the British blockade, significantly reducing its output to two-thirds of prewar levels. Daily calorie consumption plummeted, in France dropping to 1,080, compared to 1,980 in Germany, painting a grim picture of the population's struggle for sustenance. The struggle for sustenance became a daily ordeal for many, with the scarcity of food leading to malnutrition and increased mortality rates, especially among vulnerable populations like infants. Belgium also, reliant on food imports and suffering from similar constraints, witnessed the dire effects of malnutrition on its population, underscoring the depth of hardship experienced under German occupation.

6. Personal Accounts of Food Scarcity in Paris

Personal accounts from Paris in 1942 provide a vivid and distressing picture of the daily struggles faced by civilians in their quest for food amidst severe rationing. One Parisian woman's diary entries detail her futile attempts to procure food, illustrating the pervasive scarcity. Her day begins with a visit to the baker's, hopeful for bread and biscuits, only to be met with disappointment at every turn?from the butcher's empty promises of meat to the dairy shop's lack of cheese and the grocer's delayed vegetable delivery. These personal stories highlight the profound impact of rationing on individuals' lives, painting a vivid picture of the desperation and helplessness felt by many under German occupation.

7. Compulsory Labor Measures in France and Belgium

In response to labor shortages, German authorities in France and Belgium implemented compulsory labor measures. France's Military Government established recruiting offices offering attractive wages and benefits, successfully mobilizing a significant workforce for German military projects and factories. However, as voluntary participation waned, Germany resorted to more coercive tactics. By September 1942, France introduced compulsory labor laws, mandating work for certain demographics and freezing workers in their current jobs. In Belgium, the Military Government actively pursued labor mobilization, imposing compulsory service for vast swathes of the population, a move that reignited bitter memories of World War I and fueled resistance.

8. Growing Resistance and German Repression

The imposition of compulsory labor measures, combined with economic exploitation, fueled a surge in resistance activities, particularly in France. The formation of the rural guerrilla movement, the maquis, became a significant development, with draft-dodgers and resistors forming a robust base for opposition. The resistance was further strengthened by figures like Jean Moulin, who facilitated coordination among various groups and established vital services like the Bureau d'Information et de Presse. Despite these efforts, German authorities achieved some success in suppressing the resistance, employing brutal reprisals that, paradoxically, often spurred recruitment rather than deterrence.

9. Expansion of the Final Solution

During this period, the Final Solution saw a chilling efficiency in its execution, with the systematic deportation of Jews from Western Europe to extermination camps. The involvement of military and police forces, alongside the complicity of collaborationist regimes like Vichy France, facilitated this genocidal campaign. Notably, the implementation of the Service du Travail Obligatoire (STO) by the Vichy regime, mandating French labor in Germany, not only strained the regime's legitimacy but also exacerbated the exodus of individuals into resistance movements, indirectly contributing to the opposition against German occupation.

10. Manipulation of Vichy France's Leadership

In an effort to facilitate the deportation of French Jews, German officials, including Hitler and Ribbentrop, exerted pressure on Vichy France's Prime Minister Pierre Laval, exploiting his anti-Communist stance and fears of Anglo-Saxon dominance. Despite his reluctance, largely due to fears of public unrest and lack of German concessions, Laval eventually capitulated. This led to a significant number of deportations, although the rate was much lower than in the Netherlands, with about 25% of French Jews perishing, compared to 75% in the Netherlands. The SS's authority to bypass the Military Government in deportation matters from June 1942 further escalated the situation, although French authorities increasingly resisted cooperation as the war progressed.

11. Differing Approaches in Belgium and France

The extermination efforts in Belgium were notably different due to the absence of a senior SS and police leader, which meant the Military Government in Brussels set the pace for Jewish deportations. Initially, deportations focused on foreign Jews, with about 90% of the 10,000 Jews deported in September 1942 being non-natives. The Military Government's knowledge of the Jews' eventual fate and its attempts to conduct deportations discreetly to avoid public sympathy highlight a pragmatic rather than moral approach. The situation in France was complex, with the Vichy regime's involvement and the gradual increase in deportations, culminating in the arrest and deportation of 15,000 Jews in the latter stages of the occupation.

12. Greek Jews: Persecution and Resistance

In Greece, the German army's active role in persecuting and deporting the Jewish population was marked by direct and brutal interventions, contrasting with their approach in other occupied territories. For instance, in Salonika, the Wehrmacht commander ordered Jewish men to be subjected to hard labor under degrading conditions, such as standing for hours in the sun without hats, violating Jewish religious practices. This public humiliation was accompanied by German soldiers looting Jewish properties and desecrating Jewish cemeteries. The involvement extended to logistical support for the SS and police, particularly in deporting Salonika's Jews in spring 1943, demonstrating the army's complicity and active participation in the Holocaust in Greece.

13. Partisan Resistance in Greece

Partisan resistance in Greece, sparked by the harsh occupation conditions, gained momentum with the presence and actions of the Italian military. Split between the National Republican Greek League (EDES) and the Communist National Liberation Front (EAM) along with its military arm ELAS, these groups, despite their differences, grew increasingly effective. German reprisals failed to suppress their activities, leading to a wider spread of resistance by the summer of 1943. The German misunderstanding of Greek conditions stemmed from their flawed assumptions regarding the effectiveness of their reprisal campaigns, drawing from experiences in Serbia. Unlike Serbia, where divisions between Chetniks and Partisans were exploited, Greece lacked such divisions between EDES and ELAS. Furthermore, neither group engaged in tactics vulnerable to German countermeasures. Consequently, German reprisal tactics in Greece failed, as harsh measures didn't deter the resistance but instead fueled it, with Partisan activities escalating despite German efforts.

14. Partisan Strength and Ethnic Struggles in the NDH

Yugoslavia's struggle during 1942-1943 was characterized by the formidable rise of Tito's Partisans, particularly in Bosnia within the Independent State of Croatia (NDH). The Partisans capitalized on the mountainous terrain to rebuild and strengthen their forces, increasingly becoming the focal point of anti-Axis resistance. This period also saw intense ethnic conflicts, notably between the Partisans, the Bosnian Serb Chetniks, and the Ustasha regime, with each group committing acts of violence against the others' communities, thus exacerbating the cycle of ethnic violence and resistance.

15. Economic Exploitation and Axis Pressures

The economic exploitation in the NDH by the Axis powers was extensive and strategic, significantly impacting the local populace and the overall stability of the region. German and Italian forces not only requisitioned a large portion of the workforce for labor in the Reich but also commandeered vital natural resources such as oil and bauxite mines. This extraction was compounded by the imposition of hefty occupation costs, leading to the Ustasha regime's increased monetary printing, which in turn triggered rampant inflation. Such economic pressures not only strained the everyday lives of the Croatian people but also sowed seeds of discontent that the Partisans were able to exploit. Despite being a nominally sovereign entity, the NDH's economy was heavily leveraged by the Axis for their war efforts. As the stark economic disparities and hardships endured by the population under Axis policies contributed directly to the swelling ranks of the Partisan movement, as more individuals became disillusioned with the occupying forces and the puppet Ustasha regime.

16. Complex Dynamics of Resistance and Collaboration

The resistance in the NDH was characterized by a complex interplay of ethnic tensions, with the Partisans advocating for a multi-ethnic resistance against the Axis and the collaborationist Ustasha regime. Their efforts to portray the Ustasha and the Chetniks as reactionary and treacherous helped garner support from various ethnic groups within the NDH. By casting the Ustasha and the Chetniks in a treacherous light, the Partisans appealed to a broad cross-section of the Yugoslav population, presenting themselves as a unifying force against foreign oppression and internal division.

Their commitment to political and economic reforms and the establishment of the Anti-Fascist Council for the People's Liberation of Yugoslavia (AVNOJ) further solidified their position as a formidable resistance force. Their propaganda emphasized the reactionary nature of their enemies, framing their struggle as one for national liberation and social justice. This narrative was bolstered by their concrete commitment to political and economic reforms, which promised a more inclusive and equitable post-war Yugoslavia.

The establishment of the Anti-Fascist Council for the People's Liberation of Yugoslavia (AVNOJ) was a critical step in this direction, serving as a symbolic and functional body that represented a diverse coalition of anti-fascist groups. AVNOJ's role in the resistance movement lent the Partisans not only political legitimacy but also helped consolidate their status as a formidable force capable of governing and enacting change, further amplifying their appeal among various ethnic and social groups within the NDH.

17. Brutal Counterinsurgency and the Limits of German Strategy

German counterinsurgency efforts in the NDH were characterized by brutal tactics, including large-scale operations (Grossunternehmen) aimed at destroying Partisan concentrations. However, these efforts often resulted in significant civilian casualties without decisively defeating the Partisans. The reliance on such tactics, combined with the use of substandard units and the failure to sustain pressure on the Partisans between operations, underscored the limitations of the German military strategy in Yugoslavia. The Germans' brutality, particularly in executing deserting Partisans, inadvertently strengthened the Partisan resolve and support base, highlighting the counterproductive nature of their approach.

18. Ruthlessness of Elite German Units

In the first half of 1943, the Wehrmacht deployed elite units like the 7th Waffen-SS Division "Prinz Eugen" and the 1st Mountain Division in anti-Partisan operations in Yugoslavia with the aim of decisively crushing the Partisan movement. These units, particularly well-equipped and composed largely of ethnic Germans from the region, exhibited extreme brutality in their conduct. Such barbarity, including the execution of a vast majority of prisoners taken during operations, alienated the civilian population, undermining the military contributions of these units and exacerbating resistance.

19. Human Cost of Counterinsurgency Operations

A poignant account by British SOE operative Frank Deakin highlights the tragic human toll of German counterinsurgency efforts. During an operation in Bosnia, the desperation of the local populace was laid bare in a harrowing incident involving the mother and her newborn in a Bosnian cave during a German anti-Partisan operation starkly illustrates the desperation and terror faced by civilians caught in the crossfire of World War II's brutal counterinsurgency efforts.

As German troops approached, the presence of the newborn, whose cries could reveal their hiding place, posed an immediate and grave danger to all hiding alongside. In an act of desperation, borne out of an unimaginable choice, the mother was compelled to silence her child permanently by strangling, a decision that no doubt left an indelible mark of sorrow and trauma.

This incident not only highlights the extreme measures civilians resorted to for survival but also serves as a poignant reminder of the often overlooked or underreported tragedies that occur during wartime. The presence of the child's cradle in the cave still today, as mentioned by British SOE operative Frank Deakin, stands as a silent testament to the loss and sacrifices endured by innocent bystanders during such operations. It underscores the profound and lasting impact of war on individuals and communities, marking a moment of profound tragedy that encapsulates the broader, devastating effects of the conflict on civilian life.

20. Partisan Resilience and Growth

Despite the intensified German efforts, the Partisans, led by Tito, demonstrated remarkable resilience and continued to grow in strength. By the spring of 1943, the Partisans had not only survived repeated offensives but had also managed to deliver significant blows against German forces, showcasing their increasing military capability. The battle of the Neretva, where the Partisans decisively defeated their Chetnik rivals, and the continuous expansion of their ranks, underscored the Partisans' consolidation of power and their pivotal role in the resistance against Axis forces in the NDH.

21. Overall Hardening of German Occupation

Throughout 1942 and the early months of 1943, the German occupation across Western Europe and the Balkans witnessed a significant hardening, characterized by a more severe and ruthless approach. In Western Europe, this was manifested in the increasing exploitation of economic resources and labor to meet the demands of the German war effort. The extermination of Jews also became more systematic, with the Military Government in Brussels taking a more active role. In the Balkans, the German response to resistance was marked by an escalation in brutality, driven by ideological convictions and a misguided anti-partisan doctrine. This period set a precedent for the extreme measures the German army would continue to employ in anti-partisan warfare, revealing the darker aspects of its operations.
