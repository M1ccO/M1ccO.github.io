1. Hitler's Decision to Invade the Soviet Union

Hitler's paramount foreign-policy goal was the destruction of the Soviet Union, and 1941 was chosen for this massive endeavor due to several factors. Despite the operational plan and the infamous 'criminal orders' drafted by the OKH, which aimed to dictate troop conduct, no senior commander fundamentally opposed the invasion, aligning the German army with a plan marked by immense hubris and immorality. The invasion, undertaken before the German army was fully prepared, would lead to disastrous consequences, highlighting the significance of Hitler's decision which was propelled by Britain's refusal to negotiate peace, even after the Battle of Britain failed to subdue the British air force. Hitler believed that eliminating the Soviet Union would demoralize Britain and simplify the path to global domination, especially concerning future conflicts with the United States.

2. Economic and Strategic Considerations

The decision to invade was also driven by economic imperatives. The Soviet Union's resources?grain, oil, and others?promised a substantial economic gain compared to what occupied Western Europe could offer. The naval blockade by Britain, although less effective than in World War I due to resource acquisition from occupied territories, still hindered Germany's access to global markets and made Soviet resources even more appealing. The economic dynamics of the Nazi-Soviet pact, fluctuating between beneficial trade and uneasy dependence, further motivated the invasion, showcasing the strategic and economic calculations behind the Operation Barbarossa.

3. Assessment of Soviet Fighting Power

German military intelligence, notably optimistic, played a crucial role in underestimating Soviet military capabilities. The intelligence unit responsible, Foreign Armies East (FHO), suffered from a lack of resources and reliable information, heavily relying on external sources with limited insight into high Soviet echelons. Their assessments of the Soviet Union's military power were often vague and based on outdated stereotypes or misinterpretations of the Red Army's performance, particularly during the Winter War against Finland. This overconfidence in the Wehrmacht's superiority and underestimation of the Red Army's strength were critical in Hitler's decision to invade.

4. Wehrmacht's Strength and Preparations

On paper, the Wehrmacht's numbers and organization could seem advantageous compared to the Red Army. The German army had completed a significant reorganization before the invasion, increasing its division count and preparing for a widespread deployment along the Eastern Front. However, this did not account for the Red Army's ongoing expansion and reorganization, which, although incomplete, represented a considerable force. The perceived qualitative superiority of German forces over the Red Army's primarily peasant-based conscripts overlooked the rising educational standards and ideological motivation within the Soviet Union, as well as the diminishing quality of German officer training due to wartime pressures.

5. Wehrmacht's Declining Training Standards

By late 1940, the German military faced challenges in maintaining the high training standards achieved before their campaigns in the West. New divisions were not as well-trained, and the replacement pool was drying up, indicating a shift in the quality and readiness of the Wehrmacht's personnel. This period also saw an overestimation of the impact of Stalin's purges on the Red Army's officer corps. While these purges did affect morale and initiative, the assumption that the Red Army would disintegrate under German assault proved overly optimistic.

6. Red Army's Officer Corps and Air Power

The Red Army's officer corps, despite suffering from the effects of purges and a lack of initiative fostered by a political system that prioritized Communist Party oversight, showed resilience. The emergence of junior officers with real talent and the survival of several high-ranking commanders throughout the war indicated an underappreciated robustness. On the other hand, the Soviet air force, transitioning with new aircraft models and plagued by training issues, was not as prepared as the Luftwaffe. However, the Luftwaffe's advantage was not decisive due to its commitments on other fronts and the burgeoning Soviet aircraft production.

7. Mechanized Forces and Artillery

The disparity in mechanized forces and artillery between the two armies was notable. The Soviet Union's vast artillery capabilities and the ongoing reorganization of its armored forces, including the introduction of superior tank models like the T-34, presented significant challenges to the German Panzer divisions. The German reliance on a mixed quality of tanks and the lack of preparedness for the Soviet's newer models underscored the precariousness of the Wehrmacht's technological advantage.

8. Economic Constraints and Strategy

The German war economy faced significant challenges, including shortages of workers and resources, infighting among various military and economic agencies, and a strategic underestimation of the Soviet Union's resilience. Hitler's insistence on a quick victory led to an under-resourced army, risking severe consequences if the campaign extended and allowed the Soviet Union to leverage its superior economic resources. The Wehrmacht's reliance on horse-drawn transport and captured equipment further highlighted the logistical and operational challenges facing the German invasion force.

9. Logistical Challenges and Strategic Overconfidence

The German invasion force presented a dichotomy in its levels of transportation, juxtaposing cutting-edge technology with methods reminiscent of World War I. Despite some generals expressing doubts, a prevailing group-think driven by Hitler's influence, intelligence optimism, and an overconfident senior officer corps led to an underestimation of the logistical hurdles. This overconfidence overshadowed the warnings of key planners like General Wagner, who outlined the immense logistical challenges but was largely ignored.

10. Operation Barbarossa's Ambitious Plan

Hitler's F?hrer Directive 21 outlined an ambitious three-phase plan for Operation Barbarossa, aiming for a rapid advance and encirclement of Soviet forces to prevent a retreat into Russia's vast interior. The plan's grand scale envisioned a significant portion of the German land forces being withdrawn after reaching strategic eastern points, underscoring the imperative for a swift victory before winter.

11. High Command's Divergent Views and Hitler's Directives

A conflict emerged between Hitler and the OKH regarding the prioritization of strategic objectives, with Hitler emphasizing economic targets in the south and Leningrad's symbolic value, while the OKH favored a direct thrust towards Moscow. This divergence led to operational planning complications and a lack of clear objectives for the Panzer groups, further compounded by Halder's clandestine efforts to maintain the focus on Moscow.

12. Generals' Reception and Ideological Alignment

As the operational plan was disseminated among the ranks, there was little public dissent among the generals, many of whom were swayed by Hitler's rising influence post-France and aligned with his ideological stance against Bolshevism. Skepticism existed, particularly among Panzer generals, regarding the feasibility and clarity of the invasion plan. Nonetheless, the combination of strategic predicament, ideological concurrence, and professional arrogance stifled vocal opposition, with the generals broadly accepting the necessity and justifiability of the invasion.

13. Ideological Conviction and Military Arrogance

The German military leadership's overconfidence in their superiority over the Red Army was reinforced by ideological disdain for Slavic people and Bolshevism. This mindset, influenced by previous victories and historical biases, permeated the senior officer corps, leading to a dehumanization of the Soviet forces and populace. High-ranking generals openly expressed their ideological alignment with the Nazi worldview, framing the invasion as a clash of civilizations and races, further blurring the lines between military objectives and ideological warfare.

14. SS and Army Collaboration

A significant aspect of the preparations for Operation Barbarossa was the formalization of the collaboration between the SS and the Wehrmacht. This cooperation was intended to combat perceived threats within occupied territories, but effectively granted the SS sweeping powers to carry out brutal measures against civilians, particularly targeting Jews and supposed political adversaries. The army's willingness to work with the SS, despite previous reservations about their methods, highlighted a broad ideological consensus and a prioritization of security over humanitarian considerations.

15. Economic Exploitation and Starvation Policies

The Nazi regime planned extensive economic exploitation of the occupied Soviet territories, with the German military and civilian apparatus set to despoil the land for the war effort. This included a calculated policy of starvation, where the food supplies of the Soviet Union were to be redirected to feed the German military and civilian population, knowingly condemning millions of Soviet civilians and prisoners of war to death by starvation. This grim strategy was rationalized as a necessity for the continuation of the war and the sustenance of the German people.

16. Troop Indoctrination

In preparation for the invasion, the German military command intensified efforts to indoctrinate the troops with the ideological underpinnings of the campaign against the Soviet Union. This indoctrination aimed to ensure that soldiers viewed the conflict not just as a military operation but as an ideological and racial struggle, further embedding the Nazi worldview into the fabric of the Wehrmacht.

[Pages 13-16]

17. Inculcation of Nazi Ideology in the Troops

The German military command introduced comprehensive measures to embed a national-political worldview among the troops, incorporating Nazi principles through lectures, reading materials, and propaganda. The Guidelines for the Conduct of the Troops aimed to instill a ruthless mindset towards Bolsheviks, emphasizing the ideological nature of the conflict and encouraging aggressive action. This blending of Nazi charismatic leadership with the military's doctrine of independent and assertive action underpinned the ideological preparation for the invasion.

18. Implementation of the Barbarossa Decree

The Barbarossa Decree was integral to shaping the army's approach to security and pacification in the occupied territories, mandating the taking and execution of hostages in response to resistance. It granted soldiers significant latitude, diverging from international norms by allowing summary executions without trial and stipulating immunity for military personnel from prosecution for acts against civilians. Field armies reinforced the decree's impact by portraying the Soviet enemy as employing treacherous tactics, urging German troops to be relentless.

19. Impact and Reception of the Commissar Order

The Commissar Order specifically targeted Red Army commissars for execution, branding them as propagators of Bolshevik ideology and responsible for the Red Army's alleged brutal tactics. This order, distributed widely among the military ranks just before the invasion, represented a stark violation of international law by mandating the execution of individuals based on their status rather than actions. Commanders varied in their reception, with some seeking to limit direct troop involvement in executions and others embracing the order as a deterrent against resistance.

20. Consequences of Ideological Directives

The ideological directives, particularly the Barbarossa Decree and Commissar Order, had profound implications for the conduct of German troops and the nature of the conflict on the Eastern Front. While intended to strengthen resolve and deter resistance, these directives risked exacerbating the ferocity of Soviet opposition and compromising the discipline and humanity of German soldiers. Some commanders recognized the potential for these orders to intensify the enemy's resolve rather than diminish it, highlighting a critical misjudgment in the strategic calculus of the invasion.

21. Balkan Campaign's Military Parade

The rapid conquest of Yugoslavia by German forces showcased the Wehrmacht's operational prowess, with minimal German casualties and a swift capitulation from Belgrade. Similarly, the campaign in Greece, despite the challenging terrain and determined resistance from both Greek and British Commonwealth forces, ended in a decisive German victory. The fall of Crete, though costly for the German paratroopers, marked the conclusion of the Balkan campaign, setting the stage for the upcoming invasion of the Soviet Union.

22. Broadening of SS Targets in the Balkans

The Balkan campaign served as a precursor to the type of warfare anticipated in the Soviet Union, with an April 1941 directive from General Halder expanding the target groups for the SS to include Jews and Communists. This directive indicated the army leadership's growing disregard for conventional military restraints and foreshadowed the brutal policies that would be implemented during Operation Barbarossa.

23. Lessons from Civilian Resistance in the Balkans

The German military's response to civilian resistance in the Balkans offered a glimpse into the harsh measures that would later be employed in the Soviet Union. The incident in the Serb village of Donji Donbric, where men were executed and their bodies publicly displayed as a warning, exemplified the extreme measures taken against perceived resistance, highlighting the readiness of German commanders to resort to collective punishment and terror tactics.

24. Strategic and Ideological Alignment for Operation Barbarossa

Despite logistical challenges and the formidable task of confronting the Red Army, the German military's confidence, bolstered by recent victories and intelligence reports, aligned with Hitler's strategic and ideological objectives for Operation Barbarossa. The criminal orders issued to the troops, emphasizing ruthlessness and the need for security measures, reflected a convergence of pragmatic military goals with deep-seated ideological convictions, including anti-Bolshevism and the exploitation of Soviet resources.
