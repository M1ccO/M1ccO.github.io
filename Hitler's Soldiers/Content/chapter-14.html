1. Occupation Duties and Attitudes

In July 1941, the German military's attitude towards occupation in Belarus was marked by resistance, with soldiers expressing a desire to push further into Russia rather than remain as occupying forces. Their contempt for the occupied populations was evident, as they likened them to "pack animals" and imposed harsh measures to ensure compliance and productivity, such as wage cuts and forced labor. The German army's main challenge during the core period of occupation (early 1942 to summer 1943) was to manage the practical obstacles of occupation and their own harsh views of Eastern peoples, ultimately failing in the former and only partially succeeding in the latter.

2. Administrative Challenges

The German occupation was characterized by internal rivalries among various Nazi institutions, leading to a less effective civilian administration compared to the military's administration in the Reich Commissariats. Senior army administrators had more leeway to establish a sustainable occupation strategy, but their efforts were often undermined by a lack of political foresight and the enormous logistical and ethical challenges they faced.

3. Occupation Policies and Practices

Initial occupation policies included efforts to maintain order and enable economic exploitation, with some German commanders advocating for a more constructive approach that included cooperation with the non-Bolshevik population segments. However, the deeply ingrained prejudices against Eastern peoples, combined with a lack of resources and trained personnel, hindered the effectiveness of these policies. The harsh treatment of the population, combined with the military's disdain for occupation duties, only served to exacerbate the difficulties of governing the occupied territories.

4. Role of the German Military Police (GFP)

The GFP played a crucial role in maintaining order within the occupied territories, employing native informants and captured partisans to gather intelligence. Despite their efforts to manage the security situation, the GFP faced challenges due to the sheer scale of the occupation and the limited number of troops available. The early goodwill shown by some sections of the Soviet population quickly dissipated due to the brutal occupation practices, including the mass death of Soviet POWs and the dire food situation, further complicating the GFP's task.

5. Food Distribution Challenges

The German occupation forces faced significant challenges in managing food distribution due to internal rivalries and logistical issues, leading to widespread hunger and misery among the occupied populations. Efforts to improve the situation were hampered by conflicts between military and civilian authorities, resulting in ineffective policies that often worsened the food crisis. The dire situation was exacerbated by the plundering of resources by German troops and the imposition of heavy food quotas on the local population, leading to mass starvation and social unrest.

6. Agricultural and Social Reforms

In an attempt to win over the civilian population and improve agricultural productivity, the Germans initiated reforms such as the "privatization" of the collective farming system. However, these reforms were limited in scope and impact, primarily benefiting regions like northern Russia while excluding others like Ukraine. The intended boost in productivity and civilian morale was largely undermined by continued German exploitation and the harsh realities of occupation, leading to skepticism and resistance among the local population.

7. Educational and Religious Initiatives

The German occupation authorities implemented educational and religious initiatives to exert influence over the civilian population and mitigate unrest. This included the introduction of German language courses and the reopening of churches to restore a sense of normalcy and control. However, these efforts were often superficial and failed to address the deeper issues of collaboration and resistance, with many locals fearing retribution for cooperating with the occupiers.

8. Struggle for Civilian Hearts and Minds

The German occupation strategy included efforts to secure the loyalty of the civilian population through various means, including propaganda and social reforms. Despite these attempts, the brutal reality of occupation, characterized by exploitation, starvation, and violence, alienated many civilians and fueled resistance movements. The Germans' inability to effectively manage the occupied territories and address the needs of the local population ultimately undermined their control and contributed to the growing support for partisan activities.

9. Propaganda and Cultural Initiatives

The German occupation forces employed extensive propaganda to justify their presence in the Soviet Union, highlighting the supposed benefits of their rule and attempting to garner support against partisan activities. This included reopening cultural venues like theaters and cinemas, albeit with limited success due to wartime disruptions. Despite efforts to control the narrative, the effectiveness of propaganda was often undermined by the visible disparities between the occupiers' and the civilians' living conditions, leading to skepticism and resistance among the local population.

10. Soldiers' Conduct and Interactions with Civilians

The behavior of German soldiers played a significant role in shaping the relationship between the occupiers and the occupied. While some soldiers exhibited empathy towards the hardships faced by civilians, others reinforced negative stereotypes through disrespectful and abusive actions. The mixed messages from military leadership regarding the treatment of civilians further complicated these interactions, with directives oscillating between calls for harsh discipline and the need for a softer approach to win hearts and minds.

11. Sexual Relations and Exploitation

Sexual relations between German soldiers and Soviet women were fraught with complexities, ranging from consensual relationships to widespread instances of rape and sexual violence. Officially forbidden, these interactions nevertheless occurred frequently, leading to numerous illegitimate births. The establishment of regulated brothels was a controversial attempt to manage these relations, but often involved coercion and exploitation of local women, exacerbating tensions between the military and civilian populations.

12. Plunder and Requisitioning

The widespread plunder and requisitioning by German troops significantly contributed to the hardship experienced by Soviet civilians. Official policies often failed to prevent soldiers from seizing food, medical supplies, and other essential resources, leading to increased deprivation and resentment among the local population. These actions not only undermined German propaganda efforts but also fueled support for resistance movements, as civilians sought to protect their livelihoods from the occupiers' exploitation.

13. Forced Labor and Draft

The German occupation forces in the Soviet Union, driven by Fritz Sauckel?s directives, engaged in a systematic campaign to recruit or forcibly draft Soviet civilians into labor for the Reich. Initially, Sauckel's approach involved enticing Soviet citizens with the promise of favorable working conditions and remuneration, a tactic that saw some success as evidenced by positive correspondence from workers sent to Germany. However, as the grim reality of exploitation and poor living conditions became apparent, enthusiasm waned, leading to a more coercive approach to meet labor quotas. The transformation of labor recruitment from voluntary enlistment to a coercive draft not only intensified resentment among the Soviet population but also significantly bolstered the ranks of the partisan resistance, as many sought to evade the harsh and often fatal conditions of forced labor.

14. Partisan Movement and Resistance

The year 1942 marked a turning point for the Soviet partisan movement, which saw enhanced organization and support from the Soviet regime. The establishment of the Central Partisan Headquarters brought much-needed coordination, allowing for a more strategic approach to resistance. Partisan groups became adept at winning over local populations, providing administrative services, and establishing a semblance of governance in areas under their control. Despite their effectiveness, the partisans' tactics were not without controversy, as some resorted to severe measures against collaborators and even their families, mirroring the brutalities of the occupation they sought to resist. This duality in the partisan approach ? providing support and services on one hand and enforcing strict retribution on the other ? underscores the complex nature of resistance movements in the context of occupation.

15. German Anti-Partisan Tactics

In response to the escalating partisan threat, the German military devised and implemented various counterinsurgency tactics. Notably, the introduction of Jagdkommandos aimed to match the guerrilla warfare style of the partisans with small, agile units capable of reconnaissance and quick strikes. However, the broader strategy often involved large-scale operations, or Grossunternehmen, which sought to isolate and eliminate partisan strongholds through encirclement and subsequent "cleansing" operations. While occasionally effective in disrupting partisan activities, these operations frequently resulted in substantial civilian casualties and suffering, further alienating the local population and, paradoxically, driving more individuals into the arms of the resistance.

16. Impact of Occupation on Civilians

The civilian population in the occupied Soviet territories endured extreme hardships under German rule, caught between the oppressive occupation regime and the harsh realities of resistance and counterinsurgency efforts. The widespread requisitioning of resources, forced labor drafts, and indiscriminate violence during anti-partisan operations contributed to a pervasive atmosphere of fear, deprivation, and loss. This environment not only facilitated the growth of the partisan movement but also left deep scars on the civilian population, shaping the memory and legacy of the occupation and resistance in the Soviet Union during World War II.

17. Brutal Anti-Partisan Operations

The German military's anti-partisan operations, known as Grossunternehmen, often resulted in widespread destruction and a staggering number of civilian casualties, with estimates suggesting up to 300,000 noncombatant deaths in the central sector alone. While these operations occasionally disrupted partisan groups, they failed to secure long-term control or gain the trust of local populations. The absence of a sustained German presence post-operation left civilians with little choice but to align with partisans, further fueling the cycle of violence and resistance.

18. Variable Conduct of German Units

The conduct of German anti-partisan units varied significantly, with some demonstrating a degree of restraint and others engaging in brutal campaigns that contributed to the alienation and suffering of the civilian population. Notable examples include the relatively pragmatic approach of the Third Panzer Army and the 221st Security Division, which sought to clarify execution reasons and win the population's trust, contrasting sharply with the indiscriminate violence of the 201st Security Division in more strategically sensitive areas.

19. Propaganda and Counterproductive Policies

German efforts to counteract partisan support through propaganda and directives often sent mixed messages, exacerbating the confusion and mistrust among troops and civilians alike. High-level directives from Hitler and the OKW oscillated between advocating for restraint to maintain civilian confidence and endorsing severe measures that ultimately fueled partisan ranks and undermined any potential for winning hearts and minds.

20. Impact on Muslim Populations in Crimea and Caucasus

The German military's initial attempts to cultivate relationships with Muslim communities in Crimea and the Caucasus by respecting religious practices and making concessions showed early signs of success. However, these efforts were ultimately undermined by the broader failures of the German occupation, including economic exploitation, indiscriminate anti-partisan operations, and the misidentification and mistreatment of Muslim populations by the SS, leading to alienation and resistance among these communities as well.

21. Reflections on German Occupation Strategies

The overarching Nazi agenda for the Soviet Union, marked by ideological zeal and economic plunder, fundamentally constrained the potential for a more effective or humane occupation strategy. The conflicting priorities of various Nazi interest groups, coupled with the logistical challenges faced by an overstretched military administration, further complicated efforts to establish a stable and constructive regime. The dire humanitarian situation, exemplified by the food crisis of the winter of 1941-1942, highlighted the moral and practical failures of the occupation, leading to widespread misery and bolstering resistance movements across the occupied territories.

22. Failure of Policies towards Islamic Populations

The German occupation's approach to the 'favoured' Islamic populations in the Caucasus and Crimea ultimately fell short of its goals. Initial attempts to win over these communities by respecting religious practices and offering concessions were eventually undermined by the broader failures and brutalities of the occupation. Despite early successes in establishing a rapport with these populations, the deteriorating conditions under German rule, coupled with the aggressive tactics against partisans and economic exploitation, led to alienation and resistance, negating any initial goodwill.

23. Insights and Efforts of Military Administrators

Many German military administrators, particularly those from an older generation less influenced by Nazi ideology, made genuine attempts to understand and mitigate the hardships faced by the occupied populations. Their efforts to cultivate positive relations and alleviate suffering were often driven by a mix of moral considerations and pragmatic needs. The interaction between German troops and civilians was shaped by a complex interplay of ingrained prejudices, the realities of occupation, and a shared human experience, especially among older soldiers who could empathize with the familial struggles of the local populace.

24. Influence of Partisan Pressure on Occupation Tactics

The intensity of partisan resistance significantly impacted the German military's occupation strategies. Units that were under severe pressure from partisans or had high expectations from their superiors were more likely to resort to terror as a means of achieving immediate results. This reactionary approach was often seen as a necessary response to the relentless guerrilla warfare waged by the partisans, further entrenching the cycle of violence and retaliation that characterized the occupation.

25. Impact of Prejudice and Resource Strain

The German occupation forces were not immune to the deep-seated anti-Slavic prejudices that pervaded the Nazi military doctrine, nor were they shielded from the frustrations and contempt that arose from their challenging circumstances and lack of resources. These factors often led to brutal and contemptuous treatment of the civilian population, exacerbating the very alienation and resistance they sought to quell. The harsh realities of the occupation, compounded by insufficient support and the psychological toll of constant partisan threats, contributed to a fraught and often violent relationship between occupiers and occupied.
