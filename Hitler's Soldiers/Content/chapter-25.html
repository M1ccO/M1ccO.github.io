1. Dissolution of the German Army and Its Leadership

The German army's dramatic decline from its peak in 1940 to its total collapse by 1945 indicates a profound failure in leadership and moral degradation. Initially, the army was a formidable force, dominating Europe and overwhelming France. However, the leadership's moral compass shifted, leading them to engage in genocidal acts and contribute to Germany's self-destruction. This transformation was fueled by a Faustian bargain with Adolf Hitler, allowing the army to pursue its ambitions while ultimately leading to catastrophic consequences. The diverse array of senior commanders reflected a spectrum of ideologies, ranging from skilled tacticians to fanatical disciplinarians, indicating the complex internal dynamics that contributed to the army's failure. The inconsistencies and moral erosion within the leadership played a crucial role in the army's downfall.

2. Alignment of the German Army with Nazism

The German army's alignment with Nazism was shaped by various factors, including generational beliefs, ideological shifts, and strategic goals. The older generation of officers, influenced by their experiences in World War I, initially held conservative values but grew to support the Nazi regime's aggressive policies to restore Germany's great-power status. The younger officers, known as Frontk?mpfer, were more receptive to National Socialist ideals, shaped by the harsh conditions of post-World War I Germany. The text describes how the army's leadership became increasingly subordinate to the regime as they aligned with Hitler's vision of rearmament, conscription, and militarization of German society. This alignment was not merely opportunistic but stemmed from shared values and strategic goals that aligned with the Nazi regime's objectives.

3. Influence of Hitler on the German Army

Adolf Hitler's influence on the German army was profound, leading to its subordination to the Nazi regime. The reorganization of the army's high command and Hitler's foreign policy successes further entrenched his control, making him the focal point for the army's strategic decisions. As Hitler's popularity grew due to military victories, generals became less likely to challenge his directives. His ability to secure financial and professional rewards for compliant generals further solidified their loyalty. The purging of dissenting officers and the promotion of those more ideologically aligned with Nazi goals strengthened Hitler's grip on the army. The text describes how the army's subordination to Hitler went beyond strategic military objectives, aligning with broader ideological goals that led to destructive consequences.

4. German Army's Early Success and Doctrine

The early success of the German army can be attributed to its superior doctrine, emphasizing flexibility, independent thinking, and aggressive tactics known as "Auftragstaktik." This approach allowed the German army to outmaneuver its opponents in the initial years of the war, achieving rapid victories that played a critical role in the broader strategic objectives of the Nazi regime. The text discusses how this doctrine was complemented by superior organization and logistics, enabling effective coordination and execution of complex military operations. The army's focus on individual soldier training and morale further enhanced its operational effectiveness, allowing it to capitalize on tactical opportunities. These factors contributed to the German army's early dominance, creating a formidable fighting force that succeeded despite not always having numerical or material superiority.

5. Hitler's Influence on Enthusiasm and Ideology

The early years of the German army were marked by palpable enthusiasm for National Socialist ideology. This enthusiasm was particularly strong among younger soldiers, but Hitler himself inspired similar feelings across all age groups. The concept of the Volksgemeinschaft, a "people's community," found resonance within the Wehrmacht, portraying itself as an embodiment of these ideals. Careerism also played a role in motivating professional soldiers, intertwining with Nazi ideology, as both were based on the idea that the strongest and fittest should come to the fore. Despite the German army's successes, some underlying defects, such as training inconsistencies, were observed. Yet, the ineptitude of early opponents compensated for these flaws, allowing the army to prevail in its initial campaigns.

6. Mismanagement of Military Triumphs

Postwar memoirs by former generals often pointed to Hitler's mismanagement of military operations as the main reason for the German army's eventual defeat. The core argument, known as the "Lost Victories" thesis, claimed that Hitler's destructive conduct of operations from the summer of 1941 onward halted the army's succession of victories. However, the text indicates that this perspective does not consider the broader political, economic, strategic, and operational failings of the army leadership. Additionally, the Allied coalition's overwhelming material superiority played a significant role in the German army's decline, particularly in air power. The German army's failure to maintain strategic judgment and the increasing proficiency of the Allies also contributed to its defeat, demonstrating that the mismanagement of military triumphs was a critical factor in the army's downfall.

7. Faulty Grand Strategy

One of the pivotal areas where the German army faltered was in grand strategy. The text reveals that while Hitler made major strategic decisions, the army leadership had relinquished its ability to shape such decisions progressively. This was partly due to their lack of sound strategic judgment, which persisted from World War I into World War II. The departure of General Beck in 1938 further diminished the leadership's strategic insight, leading to overambitious decisions, such as the invasion of the Soviet Union, which many generals passively or actively supported despite the risks. The text highlights that the German army's strategy relied heavily on factors beyond its control, such as Japan's reluctance to support Germany against the Soviet Union and the underestimation of the logistical challenges involved in invading and occupying the Soviet Union. This strategic misalignment ultimately contributed to the German army's inability to maintain its early momentum and its eventual decline.

8. The German Army's Criminality During Wartime

The German army's complicity in criminal activities was extensive, starting from the invasion of Poland. The text outlines a long list of crimes, including the neglect and mistreatment of millions of civilians and prisoners of war, the brutal conduct of security operations, and the economic devastation of occupied Europe. The German army's actions often aligned with Nazi ideology, targeting Jews, Communists, and other minorities deemed racially or morally inferior. The brutalization and indoctrination of rank-and-file troops were also notable, leading to increased terrorization and brutality as the war progressed. The complicity of the German army in these crimes reflects the convergence of circumstances and ideology, with a harsh utilitarian view of "military necessity" contributing to its worsening conduct. This criminality ultimately eroded the army's moral threshold, allowing greater levels of brutality as the war continued.

9. Military Necessity and Ideology

As the war continued and the German army committed to long-term occupation, the SS and Police played a critical role in supporting the army's security operations. This collaboration coincided with a shift in the army's fortunes, with victories followed by a growing sense of uncertainty. As the German army became bogged down in the Soviet Union, senior commanders instructed their units to act more ruthlessly, targeting groups deemed enemies, such as Jews, Communists, Sinti, Roma, and suspected partisan supporters. The brutal conduct toward these groups stemmed from a combination of ideological contempt and a perceived military necessity. The failure to adequately supply troops and properly resource security campaigns exacerbated the army's brutality, leading to widespread plundering, starvation, and mass deaths among civilians and prisoners of war. These conditions were not a mere consequence of war but resulted from the army's conscious decisions and deeply rooted ideologies.

10. Differentiated Attitudes and Susceptibility to Ideology

The German army's personnel encompassed a broad spectrum of German society, leading to varying degrees of susceptibility to Nazi ideology and brutality. Middle-ranking officers with religious convictions and older age groups tended to be less influenced by Nazi ideology, while younger officers, with no formative experiences before the Third Reich, were more likely to embrace it. The early years of the Third Reich offered a sense of stability and security for many Germans, creating a favorable environment for Nazi ideology to take root. Younger soldiers and officers were particularly susceptible, often exhibiting more radical behavior compared to their older counterparts. This differentiated susceptibility played a significant role in shaping the conduct of various units, with elite units like Panzer or mountain divisions showing greater inclination towards brutality compared to ordinary frontline infantry units. The influence of a unit's commander, social background, and regional origin also contributed to varying behaviors among different segments of the army.

11. Crime and Brutality Among German Army Units

Despite the differentiation in attitudes, crime and brutality were widespread within the German army. Frontline units, influenced by the heat of battle, frequently killed captured Red Army soldiers, partly due to their ruthlessness and criminal orders that incited violent behavior. Elite units with younger, indoctrinated recruits often exhibited more extreme conduct in anti-partisan warfare, with significant cases of brutality occurring in southern Europe. The occupation units were generally more complicit in crimes, with the harshest conduct observed in the Soviet Union and the Balkans. While the occupation style varied between eastern and western Europe, with the latter experiencing less severe conditions, the ripple effects of the brutal eastern front impacted the western European occupation, leading to increased reprisals and victimization. This broad complicit behavior highlighted the complex nature of the German army's involvement in crimes during wartime.

12. The Decline of German Army's Fighting Power and Its Causes

From the summer of 1943 to the spring of 1945, the German army's fighting power diminished steadily and then rapidly, yet it continued to resist. This prolonged struggle was influenced by a new crop of ruthless, hard-bitten field commanders who stiffened the army's resistance on the battlefronts. The defensive skills of the units, nurtured in an environment containing experienced veterans, contributed to their staying power in defense. The increasingly harsh and ideologically suffused system of discipline, combined with prolonged combat, further toughened the troops. The National Socialist conception of warfare aimed to radicalize soldiers politically while hardening them physically and mentally to cope with the deteriorating conditions. This conception, however, also reflected the failure of the German army, compensating for its inability to win. Despite the moral implications of its actions, the German army's ability to delay defeat through resourcefulness and flexibility demonstrated the enduring impact of National Socialist ideology on the conduct of its final battles.

13. Strain on the Replacement System and War Economy

Between the summer of 1943 and early 1945, the German army's replacement system and war economy faced increasing strain. Despite the sharp decline in resources, both systems managed to sustain the field army until early 1945. Allied bombing significantly impacted the war economy, while spiraling casualty rates strained the replacement system. The text reveals that in 1943 and 1944, the Wehrmacht suffered as many fatalities as during the rest of the war combined. The German army experienced 67,000 war dead in 1940, 700,000 in 1943, and almost 1.5 million in 1944, demonstrating the sharp increase in casualties. However, even after the summer of 1944 disasters, the army still had enough manpower and equipment to delay its demise. By early 1945, the replacement system and war economy were in their death throes, with the Wehrmacht suffering more fatalities in January 1945 than any other month. This final collapse led to a surge in casualties, with over a million further fatalities during 1945, indicating the German army's rapid decline.

14. The Evolution of Why the German Army Fought On

The reasons behind the German army's prolonged resistance evolved during the final two years of the war. By February 1943, and certainly by the summer of 1943, the majority of officers and soldiers knew that Germany could not win the war outright. Fearing the consequences of defeat, they aimed to prevent Germany from losing outright, hoping to frustrate the Western Allies and split the Allied coalition. The text explains that between autumn 1943 and summer 1944, officers and soldiers harbored hopes of achieving a negotiated peace with the help of new "Wunderwaffen" (miracle weapons). However, these hopes were shattered by the summer 1944 disasters, and following the failure of the July 1944 bomb plot, many officers and soldiers were too frightened by defeat and internal terror to contemplate ending the war. As the war progressed, the failure of the Ardennes offensive in January 1945 marked the final collapse of the army's supply of manpower and materiel, leading to a continuous fight for survival. This enduring resistance was driven by fear, the influence of National Socialist belief, and the threat of terror emanating from within their own ranks.

15. Changes in Moral Conduct and Harsh Military Necessity

During the war's final two years, the German army's moral conduct became more diverse, driven by the exigencies of Germany's increasingly desperate situation. Many officers and soldiers felt justified in conducting scorched-earth campaigns in the Soviet Union and brutal anti-partisan sweeps in Italy, perceiving them as necessary for Germany's continued survival. This mindset was influenced by a stronger adherence to the principle of harsh military necessity, dismissing the suffering of non-German civilians as irrelevant compared to the potential suffering of German civilians under Allied bombing or in the event of defeat. This brutal mentality was fueled by National Socialist ideology and the apocalyptic rhetoric that became more common during the war's final years. The text indicates that officers and soldiers, seeking to continue the fight for survival, became increasingly ruthless, leading to greater brutality and moral degradation. This shift in moral conduct reflects the impact of desperate conditions and harsh military necessity on the German army's behavior during this period.

16. Infernal Feedback Mechanism in Military and Moral Failures

The German army's moral failure and military failure reinforced each other during the war's final years, creating an infernal feedback mechanism. The text describes how the army's ruthless actions, such as the scorched-earth campaigns and brutal anti-partisan operations, hardened the resolve of the enemy and contributed to the eventual destruction of the Third Reich. This feedback mechanism illustrates how the army's brutal conduct played a key role in hardening Soviet resistance, leading to a stronger resolve to defeat the Germans. The sight of burning Russian villages as the Red Army advanced westward fueled a vengeful determination among Soviet troops. This increased brutality and moral failure contributed to the German army's ultimate destruction, demonstrating the interconnectedness of military and moral failures during the war's final years. The text also mentions how this infernal feedback mechanism was not the principal reason for the German army's destruction but served as a significant contributing factor, reinforcing the cycle of brutality and moral degradation.
