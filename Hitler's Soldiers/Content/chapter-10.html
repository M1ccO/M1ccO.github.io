1. Communist Movements in Occupied Europe

In the latter half of 1941, the invasion of the Soviet Union ignited Communist movements throughout occupied Europe. Previously subdued due to the Nazi-Soviet pact, these movements sprang into action following Stalin's call to arms on July 3. They aimed to inspire resistance against the Axis powers, although their efforts yielded limited immediate success. Despite an uptick in resistance, it remained sporadic and lacked widespread support, failing to significantly impact the Axis stronghold. However, these efforts planted the seeds for the more substantial resistance that would emerge against the Germans from 1942 onwards.

2. German Retaliation and Security Policy

With the increase in resistance, particularly from Communist factions, the German military responded by intensifying its security measures. This was particularly evident in Western European countries, where the threat of resistance prompted the Germans to depend heavily on local police and collaborationist forces, easing the strain on their own overextended security apparatus. The retaliation was not only directed at resistance fighters but also included Jews, who were increasingly targeted as part of the German reprisal strategy. This approach was reflected in the actions of the Military Government in Brussels, which swiftly moved to align its anti-Semitic policies with those of Germany, despite the relatively small Jewish population in Belgium.

3. Rise in Resistance Activities

The resistance in occupied France experienced a significant surge, marked by increased sabotage and assassination attempts against German personnel. This escalation forced the German Military Government to adopt a more ruthless security policy, highlighted by the shooting of hostages as a form of reprisal. The policy of collective responsibility meant entire communities could be punished for the actions of a few, further exacerbating tensions between the occupiers and the occupied. Despite the harsh measures, the resistance continued to grow, fueled by Communist elements and supported, albeit slowly, by Allied efforts such as the Special Operations Executive (SOE) and Charles de Gaulle's Free French forces.

4. Reprisal Victims and Jewish Targeting

The German strategy of using reprisal victims, particularly Communists, as a deterrent against resistance activities, also saw a disturbing focus on Jews. The identification and targeting of Jews for reprisals and eventual deportation became a systematic part of the German occupation's security policy. This was exemplified by the establishment of associations and measures aimed at facilitating the so-called 'emigration' of Jews, which was often a euphemism for deportation to concentration camps. The situation was dire in Paris, where General von St?lpnagel's efforts to limit executions were overshadowed by Hitler's demands for more severe reprisals, leading to significant loss of life among both the resistance and the Jewish community.

5. Occupation Strategies and Burdens

The German military strategy in the Balkans involved minimal direct involvement, with the occupation burden largely placed on Axis allies and satellite states. Field Marshal Wilhelm List, the senior officer in the region, commanded the Wehrmacht forces with the intention of keeping German troop commitment low, especially in Greece and Yugoslavia. This approach led to a fragmented and ineffective occupation, particularly in Yugoslavia, where the varied ethnic and political tensions further complicated control. The Italian occupation forces, in particular, were criticized for their ineffectual administration and harsh exploitation of local resources.

6. Treatment of the Greek Population

Despite the relatively favorable view of Greeks within Nazi racial ideology, the German occupation brought significant hardship to Greece. Early interactions between German soldiers and Greek civilians were marked by contempt and exploitation, reflecting a sense of cultural superiority among the occupiers. This disdain only deepened as resistance to the Axis occupation grew, driven by the dire economic conditions and Greece's strategic importance to the German war effort. The resulting famine and suffering in Greece highlighted the brutal impact of the occupation, despite the initial lack of direct German antagonism towards the Greek population.

7. Economic Devastation and Resistance in Greece

The economic policies imposed by the German occupation led to devastating consequences for Greece, with the country's reliance on imports exacerbating the effects of the British naval blockade. The occupation's demands drained Greek resources, contributing to a catastrophic famine during the winter of 1941-1942. The harsh conditions laid the groundwork for increasing resistance movements, particularly in the mountainous regions conducive to guerrilla warfare. This resistance was further fueled by the brutal countermeasures employed by German forces, notably in Crete, setting a precedent for the mainland's growing opposition to Axis control.

8. Yugoslavia's Fragmented Occupation and Resistance

Yugoslavia's occupation was characterized by a lack of coherent control, with the burden shared among various Axis powers and collaborationist governments. This approach led to a weak and disjointed occupation, ripe for resistance movements. The Italian administration faced particular criticism for its heavy-handed and ineffective governance. The conditions in Yugoslavia, combined with the economic exploitation and military strategies of the German forces, fostered a climate of resistance. This was exemplified by the rise of ethnic Serb uprisings and the subsequent brutal reprisals, which only served to intensify the opposition to Axis occupation.

9. The Rise of the Partisan Movement

The Partisan movement, led by Josip Broz Tito, emerged as a significant force in Yugoslavia, rapidly gaining momentum and members. By early August, the movement boasted around eight thousand members organized into twenty-one detachments, primarily targeting Axis communications and supply lines, as well as collaborationist forces. Their tactics, honed from previous clandestine operations and military experiences, including those from the Spanish Civil War, proved effective in disrupting German operations and gaining local support. This period marked the transition of the Partisan movement from a nascent resistance group to a formidable opponent against the Axis forces, directly challenging German military presence and strategy in the region.

10. German Counteroffensive and Brutal Reprisals

In response to the growing Partisan threat, the German forces, led by General Franz Boehme, initiated a severe counteroffensive characterized by harsh reprisals against the Yugoslav population. Utilizing the September 16, 1941, directive, Boehme ordered the execution of a hundred hostages for every German killed and fifty for every wounded, a policy that led to widespread atrocities. This brutal approach was not only a tactical response to Partisan activities but also rooted in historical grievances, particularly the humiliations suffered by Austro-Hungarian forces at the hands of Serbs during World War I. Boehme's strategy aimed to deter further resistance through terror, resulting in the execution of thousands, including a significant number of Communists and Jews, further exacerbating the already dire humanitarian situation in occupied Yugoslavia.

11. The Strategic Shift and Intensified German Measures

The effectiveness of the German countermeasures led to a strategic shift within the Yugoslav resistance. The overwhelming force of the German reprisals, particularly those directed by Boehme, compelled Mihailovi? to distance the Chetniks from the Partisans, thereby allowing the Germans to concentrate their efforts on crushing the latter. This period saw the Partisans adopting a more conventional stance, consolidating their forces in what they perceived as a "free zone," a tactic that ultimately proved detrimental. The Germans exploited this strategic miscalculation, launching a decisive operation that significantly weakened the Partisan forces. This operation not only demonstrated the lethal efficiency of the German military machine but also highlighted the evolving nature of resistance in Yugoslavia, where ideological divisions and strategic blunders would shape the course of the conflict.
