1. Phoney War Period

The period between the outbreak of war and early April 1940 was marked by a lack of aggressive action, despite the Allies and Germans building up their forces along the frontiers in the west. Known as the Phoney War, Dr?le de Guerre, or Sitzkrieg, it was a static and tedious time, experienced under harsh winter conditions. This period allowed the German army to undergo significant upgrades, augmenting its strength and intensifying training in preparation for future operations in Scandinavia and a plan that aimed to knock France out of the war and Britain off the continent by mid-1940.

2. Allied and German Forces Preparation

Both the Allies and Germans fortified their respective sides along the Franco-German frontier, with the Maginot Line on the French side and the Westwall on the German side. Despite the apparent ease with which the Germans later penetrated Allied defenses, the forces were evenly matched on paper. The Germans, however, harbored concerns about their readiness, including a shortage of regular officers, lack of discipline in some units, and inferiority in certain areas like artillery compared to the French. This period of preparation also saw the German army grow significantly in size, with an increase in armaments production and the number of divisions.

3. German Offensive Strategy

The German offensive strategy evolved significantly during the Phoney War, culminating in the Sickle Cut Plan. This plan involved a feint attack through the Netherlands and Belgium by Army Group B, while the main effort, led by Army Group A, would penetrate through the less expected Ardennes region. This strategy required meticulous logistical planning and risked leaving no Panzer divisions in reserve. Despite its risks, the plan promised to outmaneuver the Allies by exploiting their focus on the Low Countries and their underestimation of an attack through the Ardennes.

4. Allied Strategy and Miscalculations

The Allies' strategy was based on the assumption that the Germans would advance through the Low Countries, bypassing the Maginot Line. This fixation, coupled with the French and British high command's underestimation of the Ardennes as a viable route for a significant armored thrust, led to strategic miscalculations. These included overestimating their own readiness and underestimating the German capability for rapid, mechanized warfare. The Allies' failure to properly recognize and adapt to the evolving nature of warfare, as demonstrated by the Germans, set the stage for the rapid and surprising success of the German offensive in the spring of 1940.

5. Allied Defensive Strategy

The Allied strategy hinged on the assumption that a German invasion would occur through the Low Countries, aiming to halt the German advance by reinforcing the Belgian defenses. This plan, however, exposed the Allied forces to significant risks as it required the extension of their lines and left critical sectors, like the Ardennes, relatively unguarded. The fixation on this expected route of invasion led to strategic missteps, such as the French Seventh Army's extended advance to Breda, which further stretched Allied forces thin. This strategy not only overlooked potential German moves but also ignored key warning signs and intelligence, leading to a perilous underestimation of German capabilities and intentions.

6. Armored Forces Comparison

On paper, the Allied forces, particularly the French, had a numerical advantage in tanks over the Germans, with more modern and heavily armored models like the Char B and SOMUA. However, this advantage was offset by the Germans' superior organization and deployment of their armored forces. The German Panzer divisions were better integrated into combined-arms operations, whereas the French and British lacked cohesive armored warfare doctrine and effective armored divisions. This disparity in the approach to armored combat would prove decisive in the German's rapid advances during their offensive.

7. Infantry and Training Disparities

The state of the infantry forces highlighted significant differences between the Allies and Germans. The British Expeditionary Force (BEF), while undergoing rapid expansion, lacked comprehensive training and was heavily reliant on reservist divisions that were not fully prepared for combat. The French infantry, despite being central to their defensive strategy, was not provided with adequate training, leading to a lack of readiness that was starkly exposed during the German offensive. In contrast, the German army undertook extensive retraining after their campaign in Poland, focusing on key areas such as river crossings and assaulting fortifications, which enhanced their operational effectiveness.

8. Air Force Capabilities

The disparities extended to the air forces, where the French Arm?e de l'Air was undergoing a transition that left it ill-prepared for the German onslaught. The RAF, though better funded, prioritized the defense of Britain over supporting the BEF, leading to limited air cover for Allied ground forces. In contrast, the Luftwaffe had a clear advantage in terms of both advanced aircraft and experienced pilots, further compounded by effective integration with ground operations. This superiority in the air was a critical factor in the success of the German blitzkrieg tactics.

9. Luftwaffe Superiority and Allied Limitations

The Luftwaffe held a significant advantage over the Allies in terms of both technically advanced aircraft and experienced crews. The German air force's ability to coordinate closely with ground forces, honed since the Spanish Civil War, contrasted starkly with the Allies' more fragmented approach. The RAF prioritized Britain's defense over supporting the BEF on the continent, leading to limited air cover for Allied ground forces. The Stuka dive-bomber, protected by the formidable Messerschmitt Me 109 fighter, exemplified the Luftwaffe's tactical advantage, which lacked a counterpart in the Allied air forces.

10. Operation Weser?bung: The Norway Campaign

The invasion of Denmark and Norway marked a significant prelude to the larger conflict in Western Europe. Operation Weser?bung, an interservice operation led by the OKW, aimed to secure German access to Swedish iron ore and establish strategic bases in Scandinavia. The swift and relatively unopposed occupation of Denmark facilitated Luftwaffe operations over Norway. Despite the geographical challenges and spirited Norwegian resistance, the German forces managed to secure key objectives. The Allied response, hindered by a lack of effective air support and inadequate anti-tank weaponry, failed to counter the German advance, highlighting the disparity in preparation and adaptability between the two sides.

11. Implications of the Norway Campaign

While Operation Weser?bung was a tactical success for Germany, securing strategic resources and bases in Scandinavia, it had significant long-term implications. The campaign inflicted considerable damage on the Kriegsmarine, limiting Germany's naval capabilities and affecting future plans, including the potential invasion of England. In Britain, the Norway campaign's mishandling contributed to political upheaval, leading to Winston Churchill's rise to power. These events set the stage for the Wehrmacht's launch of Operation Yellow, which capitalized on the German military's improved tactics, training, and operational planning, culminating in their greatest victory of the war.
