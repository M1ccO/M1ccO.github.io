1. Destabilizing Effects of Rearmament

Germany's rapid rearmament between 1936 and 1939, as driven by Hitler and supported by the Wehrmacht, significantly destabilized Germany's economy, the international diplomatic system, and the global military balance. This period saw a massive mobilization of resources towards military buildup, which was not entirely anticipated by the German leadership in terms of its timing and scope. The narrative illustrates the concerns among senior military officers about the pace of rearmament and the lack of a unified, effective response to mitigate its impacts. This segment underlines the extent to which the German army became subordinated to the Nazi regime, with the rearmament drive symbolizing the growing entanglement of military ambitions with political objectives.

2. Economic Pressures and the "Guns vs. Butter" Problem

The rearmament not only absorbed the majority of Nazi Germany's employment efforts but also placed enormous demands on the German economy. By focusing heavily on military preparation, the economy encountered critical issues, notably its heavy reliance on international resources and the dilemma of balancing military needs with civilian requirements for foodstuffs and consumer goods. This segment of the text highlights the regime's struggle with the "guns vs. butter" problem, which became a significant point of satire and criticism internationally, as exemplified by a 1937 cartoon in the British magazine Punch. This illustrates the broader implications of rearmament on civilian life and the economic sustainability of Nazi Germany.

3. International Arms Race and Economic Challenges

Germany's aggressive rearmament and expansionism contributed to an international arms race, not only involving Nazi Germany but also Fascist Italy and Imperial Japan. This arms race intensified the economic and political pressures on the Nazi regime, compelling it to take increasingly risky foreign policy decisions to maintain its pace in the arms competition. The narrative details the measures taken by the Nazi regime to counteract these pressures, such as agricultural reforms, selective rationing, and economic policies aimed at achieving self-sufficiency. However, these measures had their limitations, highlighting the regime's precarious balancing act between military ambitions and economic realities.

4. The Rhineland Remilitarization and Its Consequences

The remilitarization of the Rhineland in March 1936 is portrayed as a pivotal moment, both in easing economic pressures and in escalating international tensions. Hitler's decision to remilitarize the Rhineland, despite the Treaty of Versailles' restrictions, is depicted as a calculated risk that paid off in terms of domestic popularity and foreign policy. However, this move also had broader implications, reinforcing the Nazi regime's aggressive stance, emboldening Hitler's foreign policy, and contributing to the intensification of the arms race and the eventual outbreak of war. This segment encapsulates the complex interplay between economic motives, military strategy, and international diplomacy in the lead-up to World War II.

5. Strategic and Doctrinal Evolution of the Wehrmacht

The Wehrmacht, particularly its Panzer divisions, underwent significant strategic and doctrinal evolution in the late 1930s. This period saw a focus on refining armored warfare tactics, exemplified by the September 1936 maneuvers in Hesse and the 1937 maneuvers in Mecklenburg. These exercises demonstrated the potential of Panzers to spearhead combined-arms operations, challenging conventional infantry-support roles. The text also mentions the Condor Legion's involvement in the Spanish Civil War, where German tank and air crews gained valuable combat experience. This segment underscores the dynamic nature of military doctrine in response to new technologies and battlefield experiences, highlighting the Wehrmacht's adaptation to modern warfare principles.

6. Hitler's Expanding Territorial Ambitions

By November 1937, Hitler's ambitions for territorial expansion became more pronounced, focusing on the need for Lebensraum in the East. During a meeting with military chiefs, Hitler outlined a timeline that pushed for territorial conquests by 1943?1945, emphasizing the urgency due to demographic and economic pressures. The inclusion of Austria and Czechoslovakia in these plans was seen as a strategic necessity to secure Germany's eastern flank. This section reveals Hitler's calculated approach to expansion, driven by a blend of ideological, strategic, and pragmatic considerations, even as it unsettled some of his military leaders due to the aggressive timeline and potential for conflict with major European powers.

7. The Downfall of Blomberg and Fritsch

The early 1938 scandals involving Generals Blomberg and Fritsch marked a turning point in the Wehrmacht's leadership. Blomberg's marriage to a former prostitute and Fritsch's false accusation of homosexuality led to their dismissals, despite their significant roles in the military. These events are portrayed as pivotal moments that not only reflected the intertwining of personal scandals with ideological and social norms but also facilitated Hitler's consolidation of control over the military by removing dissenting voices.

8. Reorganization of the Military Command Structure

Following the removal of Blomberg and Fritsch, Hitler undertook a comprehensive reorganization of the military command structure. This restructure introduced the OKW (Armed Forces High Command) and OKH (Army High Command), with loyalists like Keitel and von Brauchitsch assuming key positions. This section details the implications of this reorganization, emphasizing the shift towards a command structure characterized by unquestioning loyalty to Hitler. It highlights the weakening of professional military judgment in favor of ideological alignment and personal loyalty to Hitler, setting the stage for the military decisions that would follow.

9. Annexation of Austria

The annexation of Austria (Anschluss) in 1938 was a pivotal moment in the lead-up to World War II. Despite the Treaty of Versailles' prohibitions, neither Britain nor France intervened when Germany annexed Austria. The text highlights the improvisational nature of the Wehrmacht's incursion into Austria, revealing both the army's shortcomings and its capacity for rapid adaptation. The narrative underscores the mixed reactions from the Austrian populace and military, illustrating the complex dynamics at play during the annexation. This event significantly bolstered the German army by incorporating the Bundesheer and its resources, further empowering the Wehrmacht's rearmament efforts.

10. Hitler's Focus on Czechoslovakia

Following the Anschluss, Hitler turned his attention to Czechoslovakia, aiming to secure Germany's eastern frontier and absorb the ethnic German population of the Sudetenland. This section of the text delves into Hitler's strategic motivations, including the desire to dismantle a democratic neighbor and seize its armaments industry. The narrative reflects on the escalating tensions and the international diplomatic maneuvers that ensued, with Britain and France initially urging Czechoslovakia to appease Hitler's demands.

11. Military Concerns and Opposition to Hitler

As Hitler's aggressive foreign policy unfolded, concerns grew within the German military leadership about the potential for a premature and disastrous war. The text explores the internal opposition to Hitler, particularly from General Beck, who feared the catastrophic consequences of Hitler's ambitions. Beck's resignation and subsequent criticism of Hitler and the military leadership underscore the deepening rifts within the Wehrmacht. This section also touches on the early stirrings of clandestine opposition to the Nazi regime from various conservative circles, hinting at the complex web of dissent that would later contribute to attempts to overthrow Hitler.

12. The Munich Agreement and Its Aftermath

The Munich Agreement, resulting from negotiations between Hitler, Chamberlain, and other European leaders, allowed Germany to annex the Sudetenland peacefully. This event is portrayed as a diplomatic victory for Hitler, temporarily alleviating the concerns of senior military commanders and deflating the momentum of the anti-Hitler conspiracy within the military. The text captures the sense of relief and triumph among the German military elite, who were pleased to see Germany's position as a great power enhanced without immediate conflict. However, the agreement also sowed the seeds of future conflicts, as it emboldened Hitler's expansionist agenda and undermined the credibility of international diplomacy.

13. Aftermath of the Munich Agreement

Following the Munich Agreement, Hitler's dissatisfaction with merely peaceful consultations became evident. Despite agreeing to resolve future disputes peacefully, Hitler's ideological drive for territorial expansion soon led to increased armaments demands, signaling his restlessness and disregard for the agreement. This section reflects on the immediate aftermath of Munich, where Hitler's ambitions and disdain for his European counterparts were clearly articulated, setting the stage for further aggressive moves.

14. Economic Strains and Rearmament Efforts

Germany's ambitious rearmament goals were soon hampered by economic realities, including a cash-flow crisis and a shortage of foreign exchange. The narrative delves into the contradictions within the Nazi regime's policies, where the push for military expansion conflicted with the need for economic stability and exports. This tension is illustrated through the impact on the Wehrmacht, with significant cuts to resource allocations affecting the army's operational readiness and leading to a reduction in production capacities for critical military equipment.

15. Expansion into Czechoslovakia

Hitler's annexation of Czechoslovakia in 1939 marked a turning point, with the occupation of the Czech lands and the establishment of the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia. This move significantly strengthened Germany's position in Central Europe but also led to a firmer stance from Britain and France, who pledged support to Poland in the face of potential German aggression. This segment captures the strategic and geopolitical shifts resulting from the annexation, highlighting the increased tensions and the complex interplay between Hitler's ambitions and international diplomacy.

16. Military Developments and SS Expansion

The expansion of the SS and its increasing militarization posed a challenge to the Wehrmacht's traditional monopoly on military power in Germany. The narrative explores the growing role of the SS in the military landscape, including the formation of armed SS units independent of the army. This development, along with intensified efforts to indoctrinate the troops with Nazi ideology, indicates a significant shift in the relationship between the military and the Nazi regime, emphasizing the regime's efforts to align military operations more closely with its ideological goals.

17. The Nazi-Soviet Pact and Its Implications

The Nazi-Soviet Pact significantly altered the strategic landscape, providing Germany with a temporary reprieve from the threat of a two-front war and ensuring a steady supply of critical resources from the Soviet Union. This pact was seen within the German military as a crucial step that saved Germany from the potential repercussions of a British naval blockade and the broader challenges of engaging in a protracted conflict with multiple fronts. The narrative underscores the pact's role in shaping Hitler's calculations regarding war with Britain and France, suggesting a calculated risk-taking approach given the perceived inevitabilities of conflict.

18. Preparations and Anticipation for War with Poland

The anticipation of war with Poland brought a sense of enthusiasm among the German military, particularly among the Junker class generals who saw it as an opportunity to reclaim ancestral lands. The narrative captures the fervor and personal stakes involved for these officers, illustrating the blend of strategic, nationalist, and personal motivations driving the German military's eagerness for the conflict. Hitler's rhetoric and foreign policy moves further stoked this enthusiasm, aligning military objectives with broader nationalist and ideological goals.

19. Competing for Resources and Military Planning

The period leading up to the war saw intense competition within the Wehrmacht for limited economic resources, complicating efforts to rearm and expand the military. The text delves into the challenges posed by the "guns-versus-butter" dilemma and the international arms race, highlighting the constraints on military planning and preparedness. Despite these challenges, the successes of Hitler's foreign policy and the acquisition of additional resources from annexed territories bolstered the military's capabilities, albeit not without internal dissent and concerns about the pace and direction of expansion.

20. The Army's Readiness and Ideological Alignment

As war approached, the German army faced the dual challenge of ensuring operational readiness and aligning with the Nazi regime's ideological imperatives. The narrative reflects on the army's state of preparedness, acknowledging its strengths in doctrine and training while also noting the limitations and the premature onset of a major conflict. The increasing influence of the SS and the intensification of national-political indoctrination within the army are highlighted, underscoring the complex interplay between military strategy, ideological conformity, and the regime's political objectives.
