1. Impact of Exercise on Mental and Physical Health

Exercise is widely recognized for its health benefits, reducing the risk of metabolic disorders like obesity, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease. It also positively impacts mental health. A large study involving 1.2 million Americans found that those who exercised reported 43 percent fewer days of poor mental health. This benefit extended across all types of exercise, with the greatest improvements seen in team sports, cycling, and aerobic and gym activities. The optimal exercise regimen was identified as forty-five minutes of activity, three to five times per week. Despite these positive findings, simple recommendations to exercise often fall short, especially for those with severe mental health conditions like schizophrenia or depression. Many patients who exercise regularly still experience significant symptoms, indicating the need for a deeper understanding of the nuances of physical activity's impact on mental health.

2. Challenges and Complexities in Exercise and Mental Health

The complexity of the relationship between exercise and mental health is illustrated by studies showing mixed results. One study following 1,700 women at midlife for twenty years found no significant impact of exercise on preventing cognitive decline after controlling for factors like socioeconomic status, menopause symptoms, hormone therapy use, diabetes, and hypertension. This suggests that the observed benefits of exercise might be due to reverse causation—healthier individuals are more likely to exercise. Similarly, a 2017 meta-analysis of exercise as a treatment for major depression, which included almost 2,500 participants, found no significant antidepressant effects. However, the World Health Organization’s 2019 report, Motion for Your Mind, indicated improved mood, slowed cognitive decline, and delayed disease onset in people with depression, schizophrenia, and dementia who engaged in physical activity. These contradictory findings highlight the challenges in assessing exercise's true impact on mental health.

3. Insulin Resistance and Mitochondrial Function in Exercise

Insulin resistance can block the benefits of exercise. A study published in Cell involved thirty-six people with varying levels of insulin resistance. After a period of exercise, those with insulin resistance showed dampened or even reversed improvements in energy metabolism, oxidative stress, inflammation, tissue repair, and growth factor responses. This finding is particularly relevant for people with chronic mental disorders, many of whom have insulin resistance. The impaired mitochondrial function associated with insulin resistance might explain why exercise is less effective for them. Athletes and trainers understand that achieving optimal performance involves more than exercise alone; factors such as diet, sleep, and avoiding harmful substances like alcohol are crucial. Alcohol, for example, can damage mitochondria and hinder mitochondrial biogenesis and mitophagy, diminishing the benefits of exercise.

4. Medications and Their Effects on Exercise Benefits

Certain medications can impair the benefits of exercise. For instance, metformin, a common diabetes medication, was found to inhibit mitochondrial adaptations to aerobic exercise. A study of fifty-three older adults revealed that those taking metformin experienced fewer improvements in muscle mitochondrial function and whole-body insulin sensitivity compared to those taking a placebo. This suggests that participants in exercise studies might have their results compromised by medications like metformin. Other diabetes medications, as well as some psychiatric medications, especially antipsychotics, are known to cause metabolic disturbances and mitochondrial dysfunction. These factors need to be considered when evaluating the effectiveness of exercise as a treatment for mental health disorders. Mitochondria play a crucial role in translating exercise into brain benefits. Exercise can promote the growth of new neurons in the hippocampus, a process essential for mood and cognitive function. However, poor mitochondrial function can inhibit these benefits, emphasizing the need to restore mitochondrial health to fully leverage the advantages of exercise.

As a general rule, exercise can help maintain current abilities or improve them, depending on the intensity and effort. To enhance metabolic capacity, individuals need to push themselves beyond their comfort zone. For those with metabolic compromises, this requires careful management to avoid risks like injury or heart attacks. The support of physical therapists, personal trainers, and healthcare providers can be vital. Overcoming the lack of energy and motivation common in those with compromised metabolism is challenging but achievable with support and education.
