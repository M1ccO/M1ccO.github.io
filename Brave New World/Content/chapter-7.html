1. First Impressions of Malpais

As Bernard and Lenina arrive at Malpais, their first impressions are shaped by the stark contrast between the familiar, controlled environment of the World State and the harsh, primitive surroundings of the pueblo. The mesa, likened to a ship stranded in a sea of dust, towers over them, its sheer size and isolation contributing to the sense of unease. The pueblo itself, with its stepped, pyramid-like structures, seems ancient and alien, a stark departure from the sterile, modern world they know. Lenina is immediately uncomfortable, expressing her dislike for their Indian guide and the unfamiliar environment. The rhythmic beating of drums from the village above adds to the surreal atmosphere, as if the very air is alive with an ancient pulse. As they climb the steep path to the village, the oppressive strangeness of the place intensifies, culminating in Lenina's discomfort with the smells, the dirt, and the primitiveness of the surroundings. This initial encounter with Malpais sets the tone for the profound cultural shock they are about to experience, highlighting the vast gulf between their world and the reality of the Reservation.

2. Encounter with the Pueblo and its People

The encounter with the people of Malpais deepens the cultural divide between Bernard, Lenina, and the inhabitants of the pueblo. As they enter the village, they are confronted with scenes that are both alien and unsettling to them. The villagers, dressed in traditional garments and adorned with feathers and beads, move about with a quiet dignity that contrasts sharply with Lenina’s revulsion and Bernard’s discomfort. The sight of two Indians running past them, carrying snakes and adorned in ceremonial paint, is met with shock and disapproval by Lenina, who finds the entire experience disturbing. Her reaction is compounded by the dirt, the piles of rubbish, and the presence of stray dogs and flies, all of which offend her deeply ingrained sense of cleanliness and order. Bernard, while more philosophical about the situation, cannot entirely suppress his own discomfort, especially when confronted by the sight of an elderly man, whose appearance—marked by extreme age and physical decay—stands in stark contrast to the artificially preserved youthfulness of those in the World State. This encounter underscores the themes of aging, decay, and mortality, which are carefully hidden or eradicated in Bernard and Lenina’s society but are a visible and accepted part of life in Malpais.

3. Cultural Shock: The Elder and the Women

Lenina’s cultural shock reaches a new level when she witnesses scenes that are completely alien to her sanitized, controlled experience of life. The sight of an old man, bent and emaciated, slowly climbing down a ladder, shocks her profoundly. In her world, aging is managed and hidden away; people are kept youthful through medical interventions until they suddenly die. The visible signs of age and decay in the old man are something Lenina has never encountered before, leading to her horrified reaction. This moment highlights the World State’s obsessive control over the natural processes of life, from birth to death, and the way in which this control has created a society unable to cope with the realities of life as experienced in the Reservation. Further intensifying her discomfort, Lenina is repelled by the sight of women openly breastfeeding their children. The intimate, natural act of nursing is something completely foreign and indecent to her, having been conditioned to see such biological functions as primitive and unnecessary in a world where technology and conditioning have replaced natural processes. Bernard, trying to assert his intellectual independence, comments on the scene in a way that further unsettles Lenina, suggesting that they might be missing out on something by not experiencing these natural relationships. This scene deepens the cultural divide between them and the people of Malpais, as Lenina’s conditioning clashes with the raw realities of life in the pueblo.

4. The Ceremony in the Village Square

The ceremony that Bernard and Lenina witness in the village square is a powerful and disturbing experience, marking a stark contrast to the rituals of the World State. The square, crowded with Indians adorned in traditional dress, becomes the stage for a ritual that is both fascinating and terrifying to the outsiders. The rhythmic beating of drums and the chants of the villagers initially remind Lenina of the familiar synthetic noises of her society’s ceremonies, providing a momentary sense of reassurance. However, the ritual soon takes on a more sinister tone as the dancers, hideously masked and painted, perform a frenzied, limping dance that grows more intense with each round. The sight of the dancers, combined with the eerie sound of the drums and the screams of women, creates a sense of escalating tension and fear. The climax of the ceremony, where the dancers toss snakes into the square and perform a ritualistic dance with them, is deeply unsettling for Lenina. The ritual, with its overt displays of primal energy and emotion, stands in stark contrast to the controlled, sterile ceremonies of the World State, challenging Lenina and Bernard’s conditioned beliefs and leaving them both disturbed and confused. This scene highlights the raw, untamed nature of the Reservation, where ancient traditions and rituals continue to hold sway, unaffected by the technological and ideological advances of the outside world.

5. The Ritual Sacrifice

The ritual that Bernard and Lenina witness in Malpais reaches a horrifying climax with a sacrificial ceremony that is both brutal and deeply symbolic. The ritual involves a young boy, approximately eighteen years old, who steps forward from the crowd at the signal of an old man. The boy, dressed only in a white cotton breech-cloth, begins to walk in circles around a writhing pile of snakes as part of the ceremony. A man wearing a coyote mask and wielding a leather whip approaches the boy and begins to strike him repeatedly. Despite the intense pain, the boy continues his slow, steady walk, making no sound. The whip falls relentlessly, and the crowd gasps and groans with each strike, a testament to the ritual’s importance and the boy’s endurance. After seven circuits, the boy collapses, and the old man ceremonially touches his back with a white feather, stained with the boy’s blood, before shaking it over the snakes. This act signifies the completion of the sacrifice. The crowd then disperses, leaving the boy, bloodied and unconscious, to be carried away by three old women. The ritual, with its mix of pagan and Christian symbols, including the images of an eagle and a man nailed to a cross, shocks Lenina to the core, leaving her desperate for the comfort of soma to escape the horror of what she has just witnessed. This event underscores the deep cultural divide between the World State and the savage society of Malpais, highlighting the stark contrasts in their respective values and beliefs.

6. The Introduction of John

Immediately following the ritual, a young man steps out onto the terrace where Bernard and Lenina are standing. His appearance is striking—though dressed in traditional Indian clothing, his hair is blond, his eyes are blue, and his skin is pale, clearly marking him as different from the others in the pueblo. The young man, speaking in perfect but slightly peculiar English, introduces himself to Bernard and Lenina, expressing his surprise and curiosity at encountering people from the "Other Place" outside the Reservation. This young man, who reveals his name is John, is deeply emotional and distraught over not being allowed to participate in the sacrifice. He expresses a desire to have taken the place of the boy in the ritual, believing he could have endured more and bled more for the sake of the pueblo and its gods. John’s yearning to prove himself, to endure pain and sacrifice for the community, reflects his internal struggle for acceptance and identity within the society that has always treated him as an outsider. This introduction sets the stage for the revelations about John’s unique background and his deep connection to both the savage society and the World State, bridging two very different worlds.

7. John’s Background and Linda’s Story

John’s background is revealed through a conversation with Bernard, where he explains his origins and his connection to the World State. John is the son of Linda, a woman from the World State who was accidentally left behind in the Reservation many years ago. Linda had come to the Reservation with a man she refers to as Tomakin, who was John’s father. After an accident in the mountains, Linda was separated from Tomakin and never saw him again, eventually being rescued and brought to Malpais by the local people. Tomakin, John explains, is the same person Bernard knows as the Director of Hatcheries and Conditioning (D.H.C.), whose real name is Thomas. This revelation shocks Bernard, connecting the seemingly distant world of the World State with the life of John in the Reservation. John, born and raised in Malpais, has always been treated as an outsider due to his different appearance and origins, which has caused him great pain and longing for acceptance. This background sets up the complex dynamics between John’s identity, his relationship with his mother Linda, and his longing to connect with the world from which his mother came.

8. Linda’s Condition and Cultural Clash

Linda’s appearance and condition provide a stark and disturbing contrast to the carefully controlled and sanitized world of the World State. When John calls for his mother, Linda emerges from the squalid house, and her appearance shocks Lenina. Linda is described as an overweight, aging woman with missing teeth, wrinkles, and a generally unkempt appearance. She wears a filthy blanket over her head, and her tunic does little to hide her sagging body. Her physical condition is a far cry from the youthful, well-preserved citizens of the World State, and Lenina is horrified by her appearance. Linda’s behavior is equally unsettling for Lenina; she rushes to embrace Lenina, sobbing with joy at seeing someone from the civilized world after so many years. Linda’s story, as she recounts it to Lenina, is one of deep suffering and regret. She talks about her struggles to adapt to life in the Reservation, her longing for the comforts of the World State, and her inability to understand or conform to the savage ways of the pueblo. Linda’s life in the Reservation has been marked by deprivation, shame, and a deep sense of loss, particularly the loss of the societal norms and comforts she once took for granted. Her story highlights the profound cultural clash between the values of the World State and the harsh realities of life in the Reservation, as well as the personal toll this clash has taken on her.
