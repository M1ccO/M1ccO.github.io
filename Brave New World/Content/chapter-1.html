1. Tour of the Fertilizing Room

The Director of Hatcheries and Conditioning conducts a tour for a group of new students at the Central London Hatchery and Conditioning Centre. The scene is set in a sterile, high-tech laboratory environment where workers in white overalls are meticulously engaged in their tasks. The Director introduces the students to the Fertilizing Room, explaining the process and its significance. The room is cold and clinical, filled with advanced equipment and the scent of scientific precision. The students follow nervously, eager to absorb the Director's words, scribbling notes as he speaks. He emphasizes the importance of the work being done here and how it contributes to the stability and functioning of society. Notable details include the presence of 300 workers, all wearing pale, corpse-colored rubber gloves, and the meticulously controlled environment for optimal conditions.

2. Description of the Fertilization Process

The Director begins with the basics of the fertilization process, showing racks of test-tubes containing ova and discussing the maintenance of the male gametes at specific temperatures to ensure their viability. He details the surgical extraction of ovaries, preservation techniques for the excised ovary, and the optimum conditions for the development of eggs, such as temperature, salinity, and viscosity. The students observe how the fertilized eggs are examined for abnormalities, counted, and transferred to porous receptacles. These receptacles are then immersed in a bouillon containing free-swimming spermatozoa at a concentration of 100,000 per cubic centimeter. After ten minutes, the eggs are re-examined and, if necessary, re-immersed until fertilized. The fertilized ova are then returned to incubators for further development. This thorough explanation highlights the scientific rigor and precision involved in the artificial reproduction process.

3. Bokanovsky's Process

The Director introduces Bokanovsky's Process, a method to induce the budding and multiplication of a single egg into multiple embryos, resulting in numerous identical individuals. One egg can proliferate into 8 to 96 buds, with each bud developing into a perfectly formed embryo. The process involves a series of arrests of development, causing the egg to respond by budding. This method is seen as a major advancement in achieving social stability by producing large numbers of standardized individuals. The Director passionately describes the machinery and the process, emphasizing its importance in creating uniform batches of human beings. The scale of this operation includes exposing the eggs to hard X-rays for a maximum of eight minutes, causing them to bud into multiple embryos. The process ensures the production of many identical individuals, contributing to social stability by standardizing human beings.

4. Role of Standardization in Social Stability

The Director elaborates on the significance of Bokanovsky's Process in maintaining social stability. He argues that standard men and women, produced in uniform batches, are crucial for the orderly functioning of society. The process allows for the creation of numerous identical twins, ensuring predictability and control. The Director cites the planetary motto, "Community, Identity, Stability," and expresses the ambition to extend this standardization indefinitely. However, he acknowledges the limitations and challenges of the process, such as the difficulty in achieving large-scale bokanovskification. Despite these challenges, the emphasis is on the benefits of having a predictable and stable population, achieved through advanced scientific techniques. Examples include the production of 16,000 to 17,000 individuals from a single ovary in some tropical centers and the challenge of producing such numbers with European material, as highlighted by Mr. Foster's competitive spirit.

5. Tour of the Embryo Store

The Director and Mr. Foster lead the students to the Embryo Store, an expansive, climate-controlled facility designed to nurture developing embryos. The room is kept at a tropical temperature and illuminated with red light to protect the embryos, similar to how photographic film is handled. Mr. Foster reveals impressive statistics about the facility: it spans 220 meters in length, 200 meters in width, and 10 meters in height. The storage consists of three tiers, each lined with rows of bottles containing embryos. These bottles are placed on conveyor racks that travel at 33.3 centimeters per hour, making a complete circuit in 267 days. This timeline ensures the embryos receive the necessary conditioning and development before they are decanted. During this period, the embryos are subjected to various treatments and injections to ensure they develop correctly, highlighting the meticulous care and scientific advancements applied in the Hatchery.

6. Detailed Embryo Development Process

Mr. Foster elaborates on the intricate processes that the embryos undergo. He explains that the embryos feed on a rich blood surrogate, essential for their growth. They receive regular injections of placentin and thyroxin, critical for stimulating development. The embryos are also administered corpus luteum extract and increasing doses of pituitary during the final stages of their growth. At meter 112, artificial maternal circulation is installed for each bottle, using a centrifugal pump to move the blood surrogate through a synthetic lung and waste-product filter. This setup mimics natural maternal functions, ensuring the embryos receive the nutrients and oxygen they need. The process also includes measures to minimize the trauma of decanting, such as familiarizing the embryos with movement and testing for sex. Embryos destined to be freemartins receive doses of male sex-hormone every 24 meters to ensure they are sterile, although they may develop slight beards. This comprehensive conditioning process highlights the scientific rigor and attention to detail necessary for producing healthy and well-adapted individuals.

7. Conditioning for Different Castes

The Director and Mr. Foster emphasize the importance of conditioning embryos for their specific roles in society. They explain that Epsilon embryos are given less oxygen to keep them "below par," affecting their brain and skeletal development to suit lower caste roles. This conditioning ensures that these individuals are perfectly adapted to their functions. The students learn about the consequences of oxygen deprivation, such as creating dwarfs or eyeless monsters if levels are too low. The lower the caste, the less oxygen the embryo receives, which is essential for maintaining the social hierarchy. This process is crucial to ensure that each individual is well-suited for their predetermined roles, contributing to the stability and efficiency of society. Additionally, Mr. Foster mentions the ongoing efforts to find a balance between rapid physical development and maintaining suitable intelligence levels, a challenge that continues to drive scientific research in the Hatchery.

8. Heat Conditioning and Occupational Conditioning

The tour continues with demonstrations of advanced conditioning techniques tailored to various roles. Embryos destined for tropical climates undergo heat conditioning, alternating between hot and cool tunnels, while being exposed to hard X-rays to develop an aversion to cold. These individuals are predestined to be miners, acetate silk spinners, and steel workers, with their bodies and minds conditioned to thrive in hot environments. Mr. Foster explains how these individuals will later be trained to love their work, aligning their physical and psychological conditioning. Additionally, embryos are prepared for specific tasks: chemical workers are exposed to lead, caustic soda, tar, and chlorine to develop tolerance. Rocket-plane engineers undergo constant rotation to improve their sense of balance, associating well-being with being upside down. This conditioning ensures that each individual is perfectly suited for their role, from developing physical resilience to aligning psychological predispositions. These detailed and specialized conditioning techniques demonstrate the extent of control and precision applied to produce a stable and efficient society.

9. Interaction with Lenina and Inoculation Procedures

During the tour, the group encounters Lenina, a nurse administering typhoid and sleeping sickness inoculations to embryos destined for tropical work. Lenina is described as pretty despite the symptoms of lupus and purple eyes caused by the red light. Her interaction with Mr. Foster, who arranges to meet her later, highlights the personal connections and routine within the Hatchery. This scene underscores the human element amidst the highly controlled scientific processes. Mr. Foster explains to the students that embryos are immunized against future diseases while they still have gills, illustrating the thorough and forward-thinking approach of the Hatchery's conditioning programs.

The Director emphasizes the importance of making people like their unescapable social destiny, describing it as the secret to happiness and virtue. This goal is achieved through comprehensive conditioning that aligns individuals' physical and psychological traits with their societal roles. The tour concludes with a brief look at the Decanting Room, where embryos complete their development and are prepared for independent existence. This detailed exploration of the Hatchery's processes illustrates the extensive control and precision applied to ensure the stability and functionality of the society.
