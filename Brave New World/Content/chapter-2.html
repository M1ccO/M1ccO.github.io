1. Infant Neo-Pavlovian Conditioning

In the Decanting Room, Mr. Foster stays behind as the Director of Hatcheries and Conditioning (D.H.C.) and his students proceed to the Infant Nurseries and Neo-Pavlovian Conditioning Rooms on the fifth floor. The room they enter is large, bright, and filled with bowls of roses. Nurses, in regulation uniforms, set up the room for a conditioning demonstration. Following the Director’s orders, the nurses bring in eight-month-old babies from a Bokanovsky Group, all dressed in khaki, indicating their Delta caste. The babies are placed on the floor, facing the roses and picture books. As the babies crawl towards these attractive items, they are suddenly subjected to loud noises and electric shocks, instigating fear and pain. The Director explains that through repeated exposure to these stimuli, the infants will develop a lifelong aversion to books and flowers. This conditioning aims to ensure that lower-caste individuals do not waste time on intellectual pursuits or nature, aligning their behaviors with the economic needs of the society.

As the infants approached the roses and books, a sudden explosion of loud noises and electric shocks caused them to scream and recoil in terror. The Director explained that these negative stimuli were deliberately paired with the sight of flowers and books to create a conditioned aversion. This aversion would be reinforced through repeated exposure, ensuring that the infants grew up with an instinctive hatred of books and flowers. This early conditioning was essential to maintain societal stability, preventing lower-caste individuals from wasting time on intellectual pursuits or developing a love for nature, which could disrupt the economic order. This method ensures the Delta caste's aversion to such distractions, keeping them focused on their societal roles.

2. Economic Justifications

A student, puzzled by the aversion to flowers, prompts the D.H.C. to explain the economic rationale behind it. Historically, lower-caste individuals were conditioned to love nature, encouraging them to consume transportation to visit the countryside. However, this love did not generate additional consumption of manufactured goods, making it economically unsound. To address this, the society decided to abolish the love of nature but maintain the demand for transportation through the promotion of country sports, which require elaborate equipment. This dual conditioning ensures that individuals continue to use transport and consume manufactured products. The Director's explanation highlights the society's prioritization of economic efficiency and control over individual preferences, demonstrating the intricate planning involved in maintaining societal stability and consumption patterns.

The D.H.C. elaborated that in the past, lower-caste individuals were conditioned to love flowers and nature, prompting them to venture into the countryside, consuming transportation services. However, this love for nature did not translate into economic benefit as it did not lead to the consumption of manufactured goods. Thus, the policy shifted to conditioning a hatred for nature while simultaneously encouraging the love for country sports that required elaborate equipment. This strategy ensured continuous economic activity by promoting the consumption of both transport and manufactured goods, thereby aligning individual behaviors with the broader economic goals of society.

3. Introduction of Hypnopædia

The Director introduces the concept of hypnopædia, or sleep-teaching, through the story of Reuben Rabinovitch, a child who, by accident, memorized a radio broadcast during his sleep. This incident revealed the potential of hypnopædia, but early attempts to use it for intellectual education failed, as students could recite information without understanding its meaning. The Director emphasizes that hypnopædia should not be used for intellectual education but is highly effective for moral conditioning. The transition from failed intellectual hypnopædia to successful moral hypnopædia marks a significant shift in how the society shapes individuals' behaviors and beliefs. The Director's recounting of Reuben's story underscores the meticulous efforts to harness technology for social conditioning, reflecting the society's commitment to maintaining control through non-rational means.

The Director narrated the story of Reuben Rabinovitch, a child who inadvertently learned a long lecture by George Bernard Shaw while asleep due to a radio being left on. This discovery highlighted the potential of hypnopædia. However, early experiments aimed at intellectual education through sleep-teaching failed, as children could recite facts but did not understand them. Consequently, the focus shifted to moral education, where hypnopædia proved highly effective. This method allowed the state to instill societal norms and values subliminally, shaping individuals' behaviors and beliefs from an early age. The Director emphasized that hypnopædia's strength lay in its ability to condition moral attitudes and reflexive behaviors, rather than imparting intellectual knowledge.

4. Class Consciousness Conditioning

The group proceeds to the fourteenth floor, where they observe children undergoing hypnopædia for Elementary Class Consciousness. The continuous whispering from under each child's pillow reinforces caste distinctions and instills contentment with their predetermined roles. The Director elaborates on the process, explaining that the repetitive suggestions become an integral part of the children's minds, shaping their judgments and desires for life. The lesson being played repeatedly emphasizes the superiority of certain castes and the inferiority of others, ensuring that children internalize these beliefs from an early age. This process ensures that societal hierarchy is maintained effortlessly, as individuals grow up accepting their caste roles without question. The Director’s demonstration of hypnopædia as the "greatest moralizing and socializing force" illustrates the pervasive control the state exerts over individuals, embedding its suggestions deeply within their consciousness to maintain order and compliance.

The Director and the students entered a dormitory where children were subjected to hypnopædia. They listened to a recording instilling class consciousness, which emphasized the superiority of certain castes and the inferiority of others. The voice under each pillow repeated phrases that reinforced caste distinctions, ensuring that children internalized these beliefs. The Director explained that these suggestions would be repeated numerous times until they became ingrained in the children's minds, shaping their judgments and desires throughout their lives. This method ensured that societal hierarchy was maintained effortlessly, as individuals grew up accepting their roles without question. The Director's demonstration highlighted hypnopædia's role in maintaining social order by embedding state-approved suggestions deeply within individuals' consciousness.
