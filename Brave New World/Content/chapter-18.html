1. Use It or Lose It

The concept "use it or lose it" applies broadly beyond physical exercise. For muscles, regular use strengthens them and increases their mitochondrial content. Conversely, lack of use leads to atrophy, as seen when a limb is immobilized in a cast. Muscles shrink quickly without regular use because the body diverts metabolic resources elsewhere. However, muscle memory allows for rapid recovery once normal activity resumes. This principle also applies to brain function. During critical developmental windows, the brain is primed to learn specific skills, but these must be reinforced through relevant experiences. Social skills, for instance, require both normal brain development and interaction with others. Neglect, abuse, and social deprivation, such as that experienced by Romanian orphans, can lead to severe cognitive and emotional deficits. These children often suffer from various disorders due to a lack of appropriate social interactions, combined with malnutrition, stress, and trauma. Their brains show reduced glucose metabolism, indicating energy deficits that hinder normal development. While some deficits can be corrected later, others become permanent if developmental windows close. Environmental influences, like excessive screen time, can also impact brain development, potentially contributing to ADHD by promoting constant stimulation and underutilizing brain networks necessary for focus and concentration.

2. Stress

Stress profoundly impacts both mental and metabolic health. The stress response requires significant energy, diverting resources from other brain and body functions. For instance, chronic stress in a child can impair learning by siphoning energy needed for cognitive processes. Prolonged stress disrupts cellular maintenance, exacerbating symptoms of mental and metabolic disorders. Mitochondria play a crucial role in managing the stress response, affecting hormone and neurotransmitter production, nervous system responses, inflammation, and epigenetic changes. Dysfunctional mitochondria can disrupt these processes, worsening health outcomes. A study on mothers, some with autistic children and others with neurotypical children, found that daily stress and mood fluctuations directly impacted mitochondrial health. Positive moods improved mitochondrial function, demonstrating the bidirectional relationship between stress and metabolic health. The Holmes-Rahe Stress Inventory, developed in the 1960s, ranks life events by their stress impact. High-stress events like the death of a loved one, divorce, or job loss are particularly impactful because they often signify a loss of purpose, highlighting the connection between stress and life's meaning.

3. Purpose in Life

Purpose in life is crucial for both mental and metabolic health. A lack of purpose can induce chronic stress, leading to poor health outcomes. Viktor Frankl, an Austrian psychiatrist and Holocaust survivor, emphasized the importance of purpose in his book "Man’s Search for Meaning." He observed that prisoners with a sense of purpose were more likely to survive the concentration camps. Modern research supports Frankl’s findings, linking a strong sense of purpose with better health outcomes. For example, a study of nearly seven thousand adults found that those with a low sense of purpose were 2.5 times more likely to die early compared to those with a strong sense of purpose. They were more likely to die from heart attacks, strokes, respiratory disorders, and gastrointestinal conditions. High cortisol and inflammation levels, common in those with a low sense of purpose, may explain these health risks. Additionally, spirituality and religious beliefs can contribute significantly to a sense of purpose. Research shows that religious practices are associated with lower rates of depression and better overall health. For instance, women who attended religious services regularly were less likely to commit suicide. While reverse causation is possible, studies suggest that practices like meditation and prayer may directly improve metabolic and mitochondrial health.

4. Addressing Love, Adversity, and Purpose with Treatment

Addressing love, adversity, and purpose is vital in treating mental and metabolic health issues. A general rule of thumb for human health is the need for a balanced life that includes close relationships, meaningful roles, responsibilities, and adequate resources. These elements contribute significantly to overall well-being. Societal factors like war, trauma, poverty, malnutrition, neglect, racism, homophobia, misogyny, and adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) can hinder people from achieving this balance, leading to mental illness. However, even those affected by these barriers can recover by understanding and addressing the impact of these experiences on their metabolism and mitochondria. Psychotherapy and other interventions can play crucial roles in this process, helping individuals build and maintain full lives despite their challenges.

5. Psychotherapy

Psychotherapy can address psychological and social factors impacting metabolism and mental health. It can help resolve conflicts with others and provide specific skills and strategies to reduce stress and cope with symptoms. Changing behaviors through cognitive behavioral therapy can also improve metabolism. For example, addressing eating or substance use disorders, enhancing sleep, and improving relationships can all positively affect metabolic and mitochondrial function. Psychotherapy helps individuals understand themselves and develop a sense of meaning and purpose, which is crucial for both mental and metabolic health. It can also provide new learning opportunities to overcome maladaptive beliefs and behaviors, such as those stemming from trauma. Therapy can "exercise" underused brain circuits, improving empathy, social skills, and cognitive abilities, provided these brain regions are metabolically healthy. The therapeutic alliance, or the relationship between therapist and client, is also essential, offering a supportive and understanding relationship that can reduce stress and improve health outcomes.

6. Psychedelic Therapy

Psychedelic drugs like psilocybin (magic mushrooms) are gaining attention as potential treatments for depression, PTSD, and other conditions. These substances can induce profound, meaningful experiences that may connect individuals with spirituality or a sense of purpose, improving mental health outcomes. A study surveyed 866 users of psychedelics and found that their experiences often led to lasting changes in metaphysical beliefs and improved mental health. However, the use of psychedelics should be guided by therapy sessions to maximize benefits and minimize risks, such as "bad trips" or triggering manic or psychotic episodes. The emerging field of psychedelic therapy suggests that these substances can be powerful tools in helping individuals find meaning and purpose, which are critical for mental and metabolic health.

7. Stress Reduction

Reducing stress is a key component of treatment. This can be achieved by either eliminating stressful environmental factors or reducing the stress response when it is safe to do so. Managing stress involves creating a manageable, pleasurable, and rewarding life. For instance, finding a less demanding job or reducing a student’s course load can help. Stress-reduction practices, many of which have been used for millennia, include meditation, praying, chanting, yoga, Pilates, tai chi, qigong, mindfulness, and breathing techniques. These practices have been shown to improve both mental and metabolic health. Studies from Harvard Medical School demonstrated that the relaxation response (RR), achieved through these practices, can significantly affect gene expression related to cellular metabolism, oxidative phosphorylation, and stress response. This research indicates that RR practices enhance mitochondrial energy production and resilience, supporting their role in improving mental and metabolic health. Therefore, incorporating stress-reduction techniques can be a powerful tool in treatment plans, promoting overall well-being and improving mitochondrial function.

8. Rehabilitation Programs

Rehabilitation programs are essential for individuals with chronic mental disorders who lack basic life skills. Many struggle with making friends, managing daily schedules, holding jobs, and finding purpose. Symptoms often prevent them from performing these tasks, even if they learned them before their illness. For those who developed their illnesses at a young age, they might never have learned these skills. Restoring metabolic health to their brains is only the first step. They need training and practice to regain or acquire these abilities. Current rehabilitation programs offer education, job training, and life skills, but their effectiveness is limited if brain function has not been restored first. For rehabilitation to succeed, these individuals need compassion, support, and opportunities to reintegrate into society. They must feel useful and respected, which requires involvement from others.

9. Summing Up

- Our environment and experiences significantly influence metabolic and mental health.

- Close relationships are crucial for human health.

- Everyone should strive for a role in society that allows them to contribute and feel valued, whether as a student, employee, caretaker, volunteer, or in another capacity.

- Psychotherapy can be an essential part of metabolic treatment, helping resolve conflicts, reduce stress, change behaviors, and develop a sense of meaning and purpose.

- Psychedelic therapy may offer profound experiences that enhance mental health by connecting individuals with spirituality or meaning.

- Stress reduction techniques, such as meditation and yoga, can improve both mental and metabolic health by enhancing mitochondrial function.

- Rehabilitation programs must support individuals in rebuilding their skills and confidence to live productive, meaningful lives.

10. Success Story: Sarah—Exercise and Finding Her Purpose

Sarah was seventeen when she was diagnosed with ADHD and a learning disability. She suffered from anxiety, insomnia, panic attacks, depression, and low self-esteem. Despite taking medications, she struggled in school and had few friends. Her family had a strong history of mental illness, which did not bode well for her long-term prognosis. Sarah had tried eight different medications, which helped her concentrate but came with side effects and continuing symptoms. She often felt overwhelmed by expectations and stayed in bed all day when depressed. Besides mental symptoms, she had migraine headaches and frequent stomach pains.

After struggling in college, Sarah found relief in Pilates classes. She began exercising regularly and noticed improvements in her mood and anxiety. At twenty-three, she became a Pilates instructor, exercising daily for hours. This was transformative for her. She reported feeling better than ever, with significant improvements in her mental health. She made new friends, started a relationship, and decided to focus on her career in fitness, dropping out of college. This decision, although contrary to her parents’ expectations, was right for her. Sarah continues to do well, taking only stimulant medication for ADHD and having stopped all other medications. She found that regular exercise, a sense of purpose, and a supportive social network were key to her recovery.

Sarah's story highlights the importance of exercise as a metabolic treatment and the role of finding meaning and purpose in life. Her success underscores the need for personalized approaches to mental and metabolic health, considering individual needs and strengths.
