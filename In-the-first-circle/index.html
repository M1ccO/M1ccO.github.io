Summary of Chapter 1: Torpedo

Set against the backdrop of a wintry December afternoon in Moscow, the narrative introduces us to Innokenty Volodin. He holds the title of State Counselor Grade Two, a rank in the diplomatic service comparable to that of a lieutenant colonel. Volodin's office overlooks the bustling Kuznetsky Most, where yardmen work tirelessly to clear the accumulating snow.

Despite the early onset of evening, the city's official machinery doesn't wind down. This extended work culture is attributed to the idiosyncratic routines of their high-ranking superior. This influential figure's nocturnal habits dictate that the whole of Moscow's bureaucratic establishment remains awake and active until the wee hours of the morning.

In the midst of this systemic rigidity, we find Volodin, a tall figure in a silky suit, visibly lost in thought. He's engrossed in a foreign magazine, but it's evident that he's not absorbing its content. A palpable tension engulfs him as he grapples with a pressing dilemma: he possesses sensitive information, the revelation of which could have profound implications. Internally, he wrestles with the decision to make a phone call that could either save or endanger many lives. The weight of this knowledge, coupled with the potential consequences of his actions, renders him anxious and restless.

Overwhelmed by the gravity of his responsibility, Innokenty leaves his office in haste. He hails a taxi, and after grappling with indecision, chooses a phone booth near the Khudozhestvenny Cinema on the Arbat to make the call. He connects with the military attaché of an embassy, presumably American. Desperately, he attempts to relay critical information regarding a Soviet agent named Georgy Koval, who is poised to receive significant details about the atomic bomb from a radio shop. However, the attaché, failing to grasp the dire urgency or the authenticity of Innokenty's information, interrogates him about his identity. As Innokenty tries to communicate further, the call abruptly disconnects.

The chapter masterfully constructs a tense atmosphere, emphasizing the high stakes of the Cold War era. It delves deep into the personal turmoil of individuals possessing critical information, capturing the essence of their moral quandaries and the perilous world in which they navigate.

Summary of Chapter 2: A Miscue

In the depths of the Moscow Central Automatic Telephone Exchange building, behind doors meant only for authorized personnel, two lieutenants are on duty in Room 194. Their main task is to monitor and record calls to and from the U.S. Embassy. The room is filled with equipment, instructions, and the hum of machinery.

Lieutenant Tyukin, taking advantage of the quiet evening, is engrossed in preparing for an upcoming politics class. He's worried about being reprimanded by the Party organizer if he doesn't present a written summary on time. To give himself time to study, he asks his colleague, Lieutenant Kuleshov, to handle their duties for a while.

Kuleshov, meanwhile, is distracted by painful sores on his leg. He's worried about their cause and is lost in thought when he suddenly realizes the tape recorder has started recording a conversation. The snippets he hears hint at espionage and the atomic bomb. Panicking, Kuleshov makes an error by disconnecting the call without noting the caller's number.

Realizing the gravity of the conversation and fearing repercussions for his mistake, Kuleshov quickly dials a phone booth number at the Sokolniki metro station. In a desperate bid to salvage the situation, he contacts "Genka" to alert the operations room, hoping they might still catch the mysterious caller.

Summary of Chapter 3: Sharashka

The chapter begins with the introduction of some "new boys," prisoners who have just arrived. The current inmates bombard them with questions about where they're from and why they have numbers on their clothing. This leads to discussions about the inhumanity of branding humans with numbers, with some expressing disbelief and outrage.

The new prisoners are informed about the rations they'll receive, sparking a debate about the quality of the cigarettes they're given. There's also discussion about the overalls the new prisoners are wearing, which resemble parachutist uniforms.

The conversations give us a glimpse into the prison system, including the various camps like Rechlag and Dubrovlag, some of which have been established recently. There are also anecdotes shared about past experiences, such as searching for a friend in Vienna in 1945 or being arrested outside the Prater.

As the conversations progress, the topic shifts to the sharashka, the institution they're currently in. The new prisoners are curious about the conditions, especially the food. They're surprised to learn about the relative luxury they'll experience, such as white bread and the lack of physical abuse.

One of the inmates, Lev Grigorievich, a linguist, gives the new arrivals a tour of the sharashka's diverse inmate population. He mentions mathematicians, engineers, designers, and even Stalin's personal architect, who's also imprisoned.

There's a lively discussion about the food, with some inmates reminiscing about their past experiences with prison food. The conversation touches on the different treatment of professionals like professors and engineers in the sharashka. There's a humorous anecdote about one prisoner who claims to have sold the Dnieper Dam to the Germans after it was blown up.

The mood becomes philosophical as Lev Grigorievich compares the sharashka to Dante's vision of the first circle of hell, a place reserved for ancient sages who weren't Christian but didn't deserve torment. He quotes Dante to describe the nature of their current situation.

The chapter ends with a sardonic reference to a Pravda article that suggests the quality of a sheep's wool depends on how it's cared for, implying that the prisoners are the "sheep" and their treatment determines their output. Overall, this chapter provides a deep dive into the prison culture, the inmates' camaraderie, and their struggle to find humor and meaning in a bleak situation.

Summary of Chapter 4: A Protestant Christmas

In this chapter, the setting is a bleak prison environment, where a makeshift Christmas tree stands as a symbol of hope and nostalgia for the inmates. The tree is a simple pine twig, adorned with a string of colorful lights, its wires connected to an accumulator.

Around this tree, six men dressed in blue denims reminiscent of parachutist uniforms gather to say a Protestant Christmas prayer. These men are in a prison room, surrounded by double bunks. While the majority of inmates are working the evening shift, these six have gathered for a somber celebration.

One of the men, Max Adam, leads the prayer. Afterward, the men reminisce about their homeland, Germany, and the warmth and joy of the Christmas celebrations there. However, one of the men, Lev Rubin, stands out. He is a Jew and a Communist, and his history is intertwined with Germany both in peace and in war. During the war, he was a major in the Department of Psychological Warfare and had interacted extensively with German prisoners of war, attempting to convert them to the Communist cause.

Despite being on opposing sides, Rubin had developed an affinity for the Germans. However, his empathy for them, after the war, resulted in him being imprisoned. Around the Christmas tree, the men share food and converse, with the conversations steering to memories of wartime Germany.

However, the conversation takes a tense turn when someone brings up the Ardennes offensive and a speech by Goebbels from Christmas 1944. Rubin acknowledges the speech's success, but SS Obersturmbannführer Simmel, another prisoner, takes offense at Rubin commenting on Goebbels. The tension between Rubin and Simmel is palpable, with the former trying to keep the peace.

Despite the undercurrent of tension, the men share news and updates. Rubin primarily speaks about the success of the Chinese Communists. However, it's evident that some of the Germans, like Max, secretly listen to international broadcasts and are more informed about global events than Rubin might assume.

Eventually, Rubin excuses himself to return to work. As he leaves, he navigates the prison's hallways, noting the unique structure of the prison which used to be a seminary. He then enters a door marked "Acoustics Laboratory."

This chapter provides a deep exploration of human connections, nostalgia, and the complex interplay of personal beliefs and backgrounds in a shared environment. It paints a picture of prisoners clinging to fragments of their past while navigating the harsh realities of their present.

Summary of Chapter 6: A Peaceful Existence

The central character introduced is Gleb Nerzhin, a prisoner who, despite his incarceration, has managed to maintain a semblance of order and calm in his life. His appearance and mannerisms reflect the impact of his confinement; his movements are minimal, conserving energy for the long sentence ahead of him. Nerzhin’s workspace is a mess of documents and books, a scene he deliberately sets up every evening as a form of camouflage to deceive any potential inspectors.

On this particular evening, Nerzhin is not engrossed in his work but instead gazes outside, contemplating the lights of Moscow. His workspace and other amenities hint at his seniority and historical connection with the sharashka. The chapter provides a brief history of the sharashka’s inception, how it was established in the outskirts of Moscow and had evolved over time. Initially, the environment was relatively relaxed for the prisoners, but as time passed, restrictions tightened.

Nerzhin's current research revolves around developing a scrambler telephone system. Specifically, he and his colleague Rubin are trying to find a method to identify unique voice characteristics over the phone. The project is daunting and progress has been slow, leading to a lighthearted exchange between the two about their commitment to the task. Their conversation provides insight into their shared history: both men served at the same fronts during the war, were awarded similar medals, were arrested in the same month, and were given the same sentence duration. Their bond is evident, and their shared experiences provide a backdrop for their camaraderie.

Rubin, at one point, passionately recommends a book by Hemingway to Nerzhin, describing the author in glowing terms. However, Nerzhin declines, expressing his desire to find his own way. Rubin, despite being uplifted by the recent communist victory in China (which he tracks on a map), feels a profound sadness.

Throughout the chapter, there is an undercurrent of tension between the need for intellectual pursuit and the restrictions and surveillance the prisoners face. This is illustrated by the scene where Nerzhin writes an insightful note about the history of Russia, suggesting that the country's swift move towards freedom was immediately followed by tyranny. In the backdrop, the environment of the Acoustics Laboratory is alive with the hum of machines, the sound of the radio, and the chatter of people.

Towards the end, there is a hint of intrigue when it's revealed that Serafima Vitalievna, a character in the vicinity, has been instructed by Major Shikin, the security officer, to keep an eye on Nerzhin.

In essence, this chapter paints a picture of the tranquil yet tense atmosphere of the sharashka, introduces key characters, their relationships, and subtly emphasizes the overarching themes of freedom, surveillance, and the interplay between personal desires and state-imposed duties.

Summary of Chapter 7: A Woman’s Heart

Chapter 7 delves into the life of SERAFIMA VITALIEVNA, affectionately known as Simochka, a young MGB lieutenant in a "sharashka" – a secret scientific research institute.
