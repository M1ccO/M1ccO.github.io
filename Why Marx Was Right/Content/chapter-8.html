1. Argument Against Marxism: Perception of Violent Political Action

Marxists are frequently criticized for advocating violent political action, an approach perceived as fundamentally opposed to democratic principles. Critics argue that Marxists eschew moderate, incremental reforms in favor of radical, violent revolution, indifferent to the resulting chaos and bloodshed. This view posits that Marxists dismiss morality as mere ideology, thus justifying their actions with the belief that the end—achieving a socialist state—justifies any means, including significant loss of life. The fear is that a small, determined group of insurrectionists will overthrow the state and impose their will on the majority, leading to widespread disorder and undermining democratic governance. This critique is rooted in historical instances where Marxist movements have indeed involved significant violence and upheaval, reinforcing the perception of Marxism as inherently undemocratic and brutal.

2. False Dichotomy of Revolution and Reform

The commonly held belief that revolution equates to violence while reform is peaceful is a simplification that fails to reflect historical realities. Significant reforms, such as the American civil rights movement, involved substantial violence, including death, beatings, lynchings, and brutal repression, despite being non-revolutionary. Similarly, liberal reforms in colonial Latin America often triggered violent social conflicts. Conversely, some revolutions, like the Bolshevik revolution of 1917 and the Dublin uprising of 1916, experienced relatively low levels of violence during the actual takeover. The Bolshevik revolution, for instance, saw key points in Moscow taken without a shot being fired, due to overwhelming popular support. These examples challenge the notion that revolutions are always violent and that reforms are inherently peaceful, highlighting the complexity and variability in the nature of political and social change.

3. Peaceful Revolutions

Historical evidence shows that not all revolutions are characterized by violence. The Dublin uprising of 1916, which led to partial independence for Ireland, involved minimal bloodshed. Similarly, the Bolshevik revolution in 1917, often depicted as a violent upheaval, saw key points in Moscow taken over without a single shot fired. Isaac Deutscher describes the government being "elbowed out of existence by a slight push" due to the massive support for the insurgents. Even the eventual collapse of the Soviet system in 1991 occurred with surprisingly little bloodshed, contrary to the expectation of widespread violence. These instances demonstrate that revolutions can indeed be relatively peaceful, depending on the context and the level of popular support. This challenges the stereotype of revolutions as inherently violent and chaotic, suggesting that under certain conditions, significant political and social change can occur with minimal violence.

4. Revolution as a Gradual Process

Revolutions are often gradual, unfolding over extended periods rather than through sudden, complete upheavals. For example, after the Bolshevik revolution, market relations and private property were not immediately abolished. Instead, these changes were approached gradually, with a focus on consensual and systematic dismantling. Similarly, the transition from feudalism to modern statehood in Europe took centuries to complete, with political power being seized relatively quickly, but societal transformation requiring much longer. This process involves not only legislative changes but also a significant cultural shift, transforming customs, institutions, and social norms. Successful revolutions, therefore, are characterized by their prolonged nature, involving extensive education and cultural change to create lasting societal transformation. This gradualist approach underscores the complexity and extended timeline often involved in achieving revolutionary goals, contrasting with the common perception of revolutions as rapid and total upheavals.

5. Historical Contexts of Revolution

Many modern states owe their existence to revolutions, which often began violently but are now seen as foundational and just. The modern British state, for instance, emerged through the intense exploitation of peasants-turned-proletarians, a process described by Marx in "Capital" as dripping with blood and dirt. Over time, successful revolutions erase their violent origins from collective memory, making the resulting status quo seem natural and inevitable. The American and French revolutions, despite initial violence, are today viewed as crucial to their nations' democratic foundations. This historical context highlights the transformative power of revolutions, suggesting that they can bring about significant, positive change, even if their initial stages are marked by violence. The notion that revolutions are inherently negative or doomed to fail is thus challenged by the enduring legacies of these historical transformations.

6. Pacifism vs. Necessary Violence

The text delves into the moral complexities surrounding the use of violence, especially in Marxist thought. While most people inherently abhor violence, true pacifism, which rejects all forms of violence, even in extreme circumstances, presents significant moral dilemmas. For instance, refusing to use force to stop an imminent violent act, such as a murderer attacking children, would be deemed grossly immoral by most. The United Nations Charter allows for armed resistance against occupying forces, under stringent conditions that reflect a global consensus on the necessity of violence in certain situations. This nuanced view recognizes the difference between necessary and excessive violence, framing it within a broader ethical context. Marxists, while often associated with violent revolution, argue that violence is sometimes a necessary response to entrenched and violent resistance from ruling classes. The goal, however, remains to minimize violence and achieve a peaceful, democratic transition wherever possible.

7. Capitalist Atrocities vs. Marxist Violence

While Marxist regimes, such as those under Stalin and Mao Zedong, are infamous for their extreme violence and mass murders, capitalism is also responsible for significant atrocities. The First World War, driven by capitalist imperial ambitions, resulted in the futile deaths of countless working-class soldiers. Colonial exploitation, genocides, and avoidable famines mark the history of capitalism. For instance, the Great Irish Famine of the 1840s saw a million deaths partly due to British adherence to free market policies in relief efforts. Marx noted the violent expropriation of English peasants, which underpinned the development of capitalist England. This historical violence in capitalist systems contrasts with the narrative that only Marxist regimes are brutal. The text argues that Marxists offer more persuasive explanations for the rise of totalitarian regimes like Stalin's and how to prevent such atrocities compared to the explanations provided by capitalist ideologies. This comparative view highlights the significant human cost associated with both economic systems, challenging the notion that capitalism is inherently less violent than Marxism.

8. Material Conditions for Revolution

Marxism posits that successful revolutions require specific material conditions. Revolutions are more likely during major crises when ruling classes are weak and divided, and socialist forces are strong and organized. The text emphasizes that revolutions are not merely the result of sheer will or courage but are deeply rooted in the material circumstances of society. Marx's materialism insists on analyzing these forces to understand revolutionary potential. For example, the Bolshevik revolution occurred in a context of severe social and economic crisis, where the existing government was unable to maintain control, and the opposition was well-prepared. This material analysis is crucial for understanding why some revolutions succeed while others fail. Furthermore, the text highlights that working-class movements, such as the Chartists and the 1930s hunger marches in Britain, generally resort to violence only when provoked or when peaceful methods have failed. This underscores the idea that violence in revolutionary movements is often a response to the entrenched power and resistance of ruling classes rather than an inherent characteristic of such movements.

9. Revolutionary Democracy

Contrary to the perception that revolutions are undemocratic, Marxism envisions revolutions as profoundly democratic processes. Successful revolutions involve the organized working class and its allies, representing a majority rather than a small group of rebels. The Bolshevik revolution, for instance, was characterized by the involvement of popular representative institutions known as soviets. Marxists argue that revolutions are about popular self-government and that nobody can make a socialist revolution on behalf of the people. This perspective aligns with G. K. Chesterton's view that certain actions, like writing one's own love letters or blowing one's nose, are inherently personal and cannot be delegated. Similarly, Lenin insisted that revolutions could not be imposed from outside, as attempted by Stalin in Eastern Europe, but must be actively made by the people themselves. This intrinsic democratic nature of socialist revolutions contrasts with the ruling classes, which Marxists view as the true undemocratic minorities. The broad participation required for a successful revolution serves as a safeguard against excessive force, highlighting the fundamentally democratic aspirations of Marxist movements.

10. Marxism and Parliamentary Democracy

Marxists do not entirely dismiss parliamentary democracy or social reform; rather, they see these as insufficient for achieving profound socialist change. Marx himself believed that in certain countries like England, Holland, and the United States, socialism could potentially be achieved through peaceful means and parliamentary participation. He supported working-class political parties, trade unions, and specific reformist measures such as extending the franchise and shortening the working day. However, Marxists critique parliamentary democracy for not being truly democratic, as it often represents the interests of private property and capital over those of the common people. Parliaments are seen as institutions where ordinary people permanently delegate their power, having little control over their actions. Marxists argue for a more direct form of democracy, where people assume power over their own lives through popular councils and assemblies. This vision of democracy is contrasted with the existing state structures, which Marxists believe primarily serve to uphold capitalist interests. This critique extends to the belief that significant socialist change will ultimately require more radical actions beyond what parliamentary systems can offer, driven by the material conditions and struggles of the working class.
