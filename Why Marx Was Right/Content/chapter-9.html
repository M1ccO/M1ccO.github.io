1. Criticism of Marxism: Belief in an All-Powerful State

Critics argue that Marxism inevitably leads to an all-powerful state, which abolishes private property and individual freedoms, ultimately resulting in a despotic regime. They contend that Marxism’s historical implementations have consistently resulted in authoritarian governments that suppress dissent and enforce their rule through fear and repression. This critique emphasizes a perceived progression within Marxist systems: power shifts from the people to the party, then to the state, and finally to a dictatorial leader. The fear is that this centralization of power is intrinsic to Marxist ideology, where the ends justify the means, often resulting in totalitarian regimes. Critics highlight historical examples such as Stalinist Russia and Maoist China to substantiate their claims, arguing that these regimes demonstrate the inevitable outcome of Marxist principles. Thus, they propose that liberal democracy, despite its imperfections, remains preferable to the oppressive potential of Marxist governance.

2. Marx's Vision of the State

Marx envisioned a future where the state, particularly its oppressive and violent aspects, would eventually wither away. Contrary to critics' claims, Marx did not advocate for an all-powerful state; instead, he hoped for the dissolution of the state as an instrument of class oppression. Marx’s idea, as expressed in the Communist Manifesto and the third volume of Capital, was that in a communist society, public power would lose its political character. Administrative functions necessary for a complex society would continue, but the coercive power used to maintain class dominance would be eliminated. Marx saw the state as a product of class conflict, inherently serving the interests of the ruling class. His vision was not of an omnipotent government but of a society where the state’s role as a tool of oppression would be rendered obsolete. This transformation would involve maintaining necessary administrative functions, such as public services and infrastructure, while removing the state’s ability to enforce class-based inequalities.

3. Distinction Between State Functions

Marx distinguished between class-specific and class-neutral functions of the state. Class-specific functions serve the interests of the ruling class, such as using police and military forces to suppress labor strikes and protests. Class-neutral functions, on the other hand, serve the general population, such as preventing crime and providing public services. Marx did not oppose the state’s role in maintaining social order and protecting individuals from harm; he supported state interventions that improved social conditions, like legislation for social welfare and public health. For example, police preventing racist violence or dedicated facilities for rape victims are seen as necessary protective roles. Marx’s critique focused on the state’s role in perpetuating class divisions and supporting elite interests while recognizing its potential to act as a force for good when not serving those interests. He believed that the state could be a powerful tool for improving social conditions, as long as it was not primarily an instrument of class oppression.

4. Role of the State in Class Conflict

Marx viewed the state as inherently partisan, defending the interests of the ruling class against those seeking social transformation. The state is not a neutral mediator in the conflict between labor and capital; it actively upholds the existing social order. This perspective challenges the liberal notion of the state as an impartial entity that balances competing social interests. Historical examples, such as the violent suppression of strikes and protests, illustrate the state’s role in protecting capitalist interests. Marx argued that the state’s primary function is to maintain the status quo and suppress revolutionary movements. For instance, the liberal state remains neutral until the critics of capitalism gain significant traction, at which point it resorts to violent suppression using police, paramilitary squads, and, if necessary, military force. Marx’s analysis highlights the contradiction between the state’s portrayal as a unifying force and its actual function as an enforcer of class dominance, seeking to prevent any fundamental change to the capitalist system.

5. Marx’s Critique of State Neutrality

Marx critiqued the idea of the state as a harmonious entity that unites different social groups. He argued that the state exacerbates social divisions by defending the interests of the ruling class. The state, rather than being a source of unity, often serves to deepen existing inequalities. Marx’s realism about the state’s role in class struggle reveals the underlying partisanship in its actions. He believed that the state’s apparent neutrality is a myth, as it ultimately serves the interests of those in power. This critique is supported by contemporary observations, such as those of Jacques Rancière, who notes that the alignment of politics with the management of capital is now openly acknowledged. Marx’s critique remains relevant in understanding the state’s role in maintaining social inequalities. He argued that the state projects an image of social harmony, while in reality, it enforces the dominance of the ruling class. This perspective challenges the liberal view of the state as a neutral arbiter, highlighting its active role in perpetuating class-based injustices.

6. Role of the State in Enforcing Social Order

Marx acknowledged that not all state functions are oppressive or class-specific. For instance, police actions that prevent racist violence or child abuse are necessary for maintaining social order and protecting individuals. Marx supported such protective roles, recognizing that as long as human freedom exists, there will be abuses requiring state intervention. Prisons, although often criticized for penalizing the socially deprived, also serve to protect society from dangerous individuals. Marx believed that the state could act as a powerful force for good, as seen in his support for legislation improving social conditions in Victorian England. This perspective highlights the dual nature of the state, which can both uphold oppressive structures and contribute positively to societal well-being.

7. The State and Class Privilege

Marx viewed the state as inherently linked to class privilege, serving primarily to maintain the dominance of the ruling class. This perspective is echoed by thinkers like Jean-Jacques Rousseau, who argued that the state fixes property and inequality, binding new fetters on the poor while giving new powers to the rich. Marx’s critique extends to the notion that the state, far from being a source of harmony, actually perpetuates division by defending the interests of the wealthy. Even though he initially held more positive views of the state as a young disciple of Hegel, Marx's later writings reflect a deep skepticism of the state’s role in perpetuating class divisions. He argued that the state, rather than promoting societal harmony, often exacerbates inequalities by upholding the interests of the governing class.

8. Marx’s Concept of Democracy

Marx advocated for a form of democracy that goes beyond parliamentary systems. He believed in the extension of democracy to all aspects of civil society, including economic life. For Marx, true democracy meant self-government by the people, not merely entrusting governance to a political elite. He envisioned a society where political power is spread across local, popular institutions rather than centralized in a distant government. This vision of democracy is grounded in popular sovereignty and the collective exercise of power. Marx saw the existing state as disconnected from civil society, projecting an image of seamless social unity while in reality, society was deeply divided. He aimed to close the gap between state and society by dissolving the state into everyday life, a process he referred to as true democracy.

9. Economic Power vs. Political Power

Marx argued that real power lies with economic institutions, such as banks and corporations, rather than with the state. These institutions, whose directors are unelected, wield significant influence over millions of lives. The state, according to Marx, is often the obedient servant of these economic powers. Governments might challenge these institutions occasionally, but if they seriously threatened their interests, they would likely face severe consequences. This dynamic limits the state’s ability to enact meaningful socialist changes. Marx asserted that the working class could not simply take over the existing state machinery because it is inherently biased towards maintaining the status quo. This critique underscores the need for a fundamental transformation of both political and economic structures to achieve true democracy and social justice.

10. The Paris Commune as a Model

Marx’s model for popular self-government was the Paris Commune of 1871, where working people took control of their destiny. The Commune comprised local councillors elected by popular vote, who could be recalled by their constituents. Public service was performed at workmen’s wages, the standing army was abolished, and the police were made accountable to the Commune. Educational institutions were opened to the common people and freed from church and state interference. This model represented a radical departure from the traditional state, embodying the principles of self-governance and popular democracy. Marx described the Commune as the “dictatorship of the proletariat,” meaning rule by the majority. This concept, often misunderstood as authoritarian, actually represented a form of popular democracy. The Commune’s practices, such as elective and accountable public officials, reflected Marx’s vision of a state that serves the people rather than a ruling elite.
