1. Argument: Marxism's Relevance

Marxism, often declared as outdated, still remains highly relevant due to its critical analysis of capitalism. Although originally associated with a world of industrial factories and massed working classes, it remains pertinent because it offers the most thorough critique of capitalism ever conceived. Despite claims that modern, socially mobile, postindustrial Western societies render Marxism obsolete, Marxism's goal is to critique and transform capitalism. This critique persists as long as capitalism exists, adapting to new forms such as financial, monopoly, and imperial capital. The dynamic nature of capitalism, which includes globalization and the decline of traditional working classes, was foreseen by Marx, further underscoring the adaptability and enduring relevance of Marxist critique. For instance, in 2001, the World Bank reported that 2.74 billion people lived on less than two dollars a day, highlighting the persistent inequality that Marxism critiques

2. Transformation of Western Societies

The Western capitalist system underwent significant transformations from the mid-1970s onwards. Traditional industrial manufacturing gave way to a postindustrial culture characterized by consumerism, information technology, and service industries. Deregulation of markets and political assaults on the working-class movement weakened traditional class allegiances, leading to an emphasis on local, gender, and ethnic identities. These changes facilitated the global expansion of transnational corporations, redistributing production and investment for maximum profit. This globalization led to massive labor migrations and a resurgence of racism and fascism as immigrants moved to advanced economies. Simultaneously, peripheral countries experienced harsh labor conditions and inequitable trade terms while the capitalist metropolises emphasized the spiritual well-being of employees amidst growing inequality. For example, the income of a single Mexican billionaire is equivalent to the earnings of the poorest 17 million of his compatriots

3. Capitalism's Evolution

Capitalism's evolution from industrial to postindustrial forms did not signify its end but rather a response to economic crises. The shift towards service, financial, and communication industries was driven by a prolonged economic downturn, leading to intensified international competition and a reduction in profit rates. The era of Reagan and Thatcher marked a period of deregulation, market freedom, and increased state repression, dismantling traditional manufacturing and weakening labor movements. This transformation, marked by apparent prosperity, masked underlying anxieties within the system, revealing a deep-rooted instability. The displacement of traditional industries and the rise of new forms of capitalism underscore its dynamic nature and the continuous relevance of Marxist critique in understanding these changes. 4. Marxist Theory and Modern Changes

Marxist theory remains relevant in analyzing contemporary capitalism, characterized by spectacular inequalities and intensified exploitation. Today's world, marked by wealth disparities, imperial warfare, and repressive states, aligns closely with the issues Marxism has addressed for centuries. The formation of urban working classes from uprooted peasantry, a process witnessed in countries like China and Brazil, reflects historical patterns Marx analyzed. The global slums documented by Mike Davis echo Engels' observations on the working class, highlighting the persistent relevance of Marxist critique in understanding modern economic and social phenomena. This continuity of issues underscores the enduring importance of Marxism in offering insights into capitalism's inherent contradictions and social impacts. For instance, as Marx predicted, the inequalities of wealth have dramatically deepened, with spectacular disparities such as the income of a single Mexican billionaire being equivalent to the earnings of the poorest 17 million of his compatriots
